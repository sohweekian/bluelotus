⚠ Canary Exclusion Registry

Why These Economies Are Not Canaries

CivilisationOS applies a strict 5-criterion selection filter. Every excluded economy has a documented reason. As circumstances change — regimes fall, reforms take hold, financial markets open — these exclusions will be revisited and upgraded.

The Selection Principle: A canary earns a slot if it passes ≥2 of 5 criteria:
S — Systemic Weight  ·  C — Capital Flow Chokepoint  ·  K — Commodity Anchor
G — Geopolitical Transmission  ·  R — Regional Signal

Economies failing <2 criteria are excluded. Their exclusion is not a political judgment — it is a signal quality and intelligence yield determination.
EXCLUSION CATEGORIES
🚫
Category 1 — Structurally Impossible

No functioning central bank, under comprehensive sanctions, failed state, or command economy with no cross-border capital flows. Permanently excluded until regime or system changes.

📡
Category 2 — Signal Already Captured

Economy's monetary policy is mechanically determined by an existing canary (USD peg follows FED, EUR peg follows ECB, INR peg follows RBI). Building a separate canary adds noise, not signal.

⏳
Category 3 — Too Small / Too Early

Economy is too small for global capital flow significance, or is too early-stage in financial openness to generate reliable kill condition signals. Round 2 or Round 3 candidates.

🔧
Category 4 — Deferred for Design Complexity

Economy is important but kill condition architecture is too complex to model correctly in a bloc-speed build. Requires a dedicated design session. Priority Round 2 additions.

🏔 Central Asia
Included: None — entire region currently excluded
Three structural reasons apply across the entire Central Asian bloc: (1) Every Central Asian economy is a tributary of two larger signals already in the system — Russia (CBR) and China (PBOC). A Central Asian canary largely re-reads those two canaries with added data noise. (2) None of the five has a meaningfully liquid Eurobond market or freely convertible currency with genuine two-way institutional flows. (3) All five central banks operate under governments with varying degrees of authoritarian control, making published monetary data difficult to independently cross-verify.
