The Agentic Production Function

Human-AI Collaboration as the New Unit of Capital Formation

Abstract

The emergence of agentic AI systems — AI models capable of autonomous multi-step task execution, tool use, iterative self-correction, and persistent goal pursuit without per-step human supervision — represents a phase transition in the economics of knowledge production, not merely an incremental productivity instrument. This dissertation introduces the Agentic Production Function (APF) as a formal extension of classical production theory designed to accommodate the qualitatively new category of agentic labour. The APF distinguishes three factor inputs: human labour (\(L_h\)), agentic AI labour (\(L_a\)), and orchestration capital (\(\Omega\)) — the governance, specification, and verification capacity required to direct agentic systems productively. The core theoretical claim is that \(L_h\) and \(L_a\) exhibit variable and context-dependent elasticity of substitution \(\sigma(L_h, L_a)\): they are complementary at high task complexity and novel problem types (where human specification and verification remain essential), substitutable at moderate task complexity and well-defined problem types (where agentic systems can execute the full task loop without human intervention), and correlated at the governance layer (where human orchestration quality determines the productive capacity of both human and agentic labour).

Drawing on five integrated research methods — quantitative productivity analysis across 312 software engineering teams (2023–2026), qualitative interviews with 28 senior software engineers and engineering leaders, 16-week ethnographic observation within a fintech team transitioning to agentic workflows, a structured superforecasting tournament on SE labour market evolution (2026–2030), and an adversarial collaborative debate between the Displacement and Augmentation schools — this dissertation finds that agentic AI integration produces a median 34% cycle time reduction and 28% defect rate reduction in well-defined engineering tasks, while simultaneously creating new task categories ("specification engineering," "agentic governance," "AI output verification") that partially absorb displaced labour from implementation tasks. The adversarial collaborative exercise produces the Conditional Displacement Hypothesis: AI agency substitutes human labour in implementation-class tasks at rates consistent with displacement concern, while generating genuinely new orchestration and verification roles that are meaningfully different from — not merely renaming of — the displaced tasks. The net labour market effect is conditional on the pace of new role emergence relative to displacement velocity, which varies substantially by sector, firm size, and task domain.

The BlueLotus V3 architecture is examined as an empirical instantiation of the optimal human-AI production configuration: five autonomous daemons (agentic labour) handle intelligence gathering and signal processing, while the human CIO (orchestration capital plus human labour) handles specification, governance, and consequential decision-making. The CIO_ONLY_MANUAL doctrine — which formally reserves strategic authority to the human decision-maker — is interpreted as an operationalised expression of the APF's substitutability boundary: the architecture knows where \(L_h\) and \(L_a\) are complements and has designed the boundary accordingly.

Keywords: agentic AI, production function, labour substitution, human-AI collaboration, agentic labour, software engineering, Claude Code, capital formation, orchestration, Minimum Viable Role, CIO_ONLY_MANUAL

Chapter 1: Introduction

1.1 The Agentic Turn

In June 2026, the BlueLotus V3 intelligence system published "The Agentic Turn" — a research thesis arguing that Claude Code and analogous agentic AI systems represent not a productivity increment in software engineering but a phase transition in how software is produced. The thesis's core claim: when an AI system can independently plan a coding task, select appropriate tools, write code, run tests, interpret errors, revise its approach, and commit a working solution — all without per-step human approval — the unit of software production is no longer the human-hours required to implement a specification but the orchestration quality required to direct the agentic system toward correct specifications.

This is the Agentic Turn: the shift from implementation as the scarce resource to specification and governance as the scarce resource. It is a phase transition rather than an increment because it changes what is valuable — not faster horses but automobiles — and therefore changes which human capabilities are economically rewarded, which are displaced, and which must be newly developed.

This dissertation provides the economic, sociological, and predictive intellectual infrastructure for the Agentic Turn thesis. It asks: What does classical production theory need to become in order to accommodate agentic AI as a factor of production? How does the phenomenology of software work change when the primary collaborator is an agentic system? What does the labour market actually look like in organisations that have integrated agentic workflows, and what does it predict for those that haven't yet? How do we forecast the distributional consequences of agentic displacement across skill categories, sectors, and time horizons? And what is the truth — or the bounded truth — behind both the displacement concern and the augmentation optimism?

1.2 Why Classical Production Theory Is Insufficient

The classical Solow production function \(Y = A \cdot f(K, L)\) treats labour as a homogeneous factor and technology as a productivity multiplier (\(A\), total factor productivity). This framework was adequate when technology was embodied in physical capital — machines, computers — that amplified human labour without substituting for its cognitive content. A faster computer allows a software engineer to compile faster; it does not write the code.

Agentic AI breaks this model in two ways. First, it introduces a new factor of production — agentic labour — that is qualitatively distinct from both physical capital and human labour: it is not a tool the human uses (capital) but an autonomous agent that can complete multi-step cognitive tasks independently (quasi-labour). Second, it renders the labour aggregate heterogeneous in a new dimension: human labour applied to implementation tasks and human labour applied to specification and governance of agentic systems are not the same input — they require different skills, produce different outputs, and have different elasticities of substitution with \(L_a\).

The Agentic Production Function proposed here addresses both insufficiencies: it disaggregates labour into human (\(L_h\)) and agentic (\(L_a\)) components, introduces orchestration capital (\(\Omega\)) as the governance capacity required to direct agentic labour productively, and specifies the variable substitution elasticity \(\sigma(L_h, L_a)\) as the key parameter that determines whether integration is complementary or substitutive in specific task contexts.

1.3 The BlueLotus V3 Empirical Anchor

The BlueLotus V3 intelligence system provides a real-world empirical anchor for the APF framework. The system's architecture — five autonomous AI daemons as agentic labour, one human CIO as orchestration capital plus human labour — is a designed instantiation of the optimal human-AI production configuration that the APF predicts. The CIO_ONLY_MANUAL doctrine, which formally reserves strategic decision authority to the human CIO while assigning execution to daemons, is an operationalised boundary condition at the substitutability edge: agentic labour executes intelligence gathering; human labour specifies what intelligence is needed and what to do with it.

This architecture did not emerge from economic theory — it emerged from practical governance requirements. The fact that it converges on the APF's theoretical optimum is evidence that organisations facing genuine agentic integration challenges discover the same production boundary through experience that economic theory would predict from first principles.

1.4 Research Questions

RQ1: Does agentic AI integration produce statistically significant improvements in software engineering productivity metrics (cycle time, defect rate, deployment frequency), and how are these gains distributed across task complexity levels?

RQ2: How does the phenomenological experience of software engineering work change when the primary collaborator is an agentic AI system, and what new cognitive and social practices emerge?

RQ3: What are the cultural and institutional dynamics of agentic workflow adoption in software teams, and how do established team rituals (standups, code reviews, retrospectives) transform?

RQ4: What calibrated probability estimates do AI-assisted superforecasters assign to key SE labour market outcomes (2026–2030), and where do AI-assisted estimates diverge most from unaided expert forecasts?

RQ5: Under what conditions does agentic AI substitute for rather than augment human SE labour, and what governance and policy frameworks can optimise the distribution of benefits and costs?

Chapter 2: Literature Review

2.1 Production Functions and Technology

The Solow (1956) growth model established the theoretical framework for treating technology as an exogenous productivity multiplier. Arrow's (1962) learning-by-doing model introduced the insight that productivity (\(A\)) is itself a function of cumulative production experience, endogenising technology. Romer's (1986, 1990) endogenous growth theory extended this to treat knowledge as a non-rival factor of production whose accumulation drives long-run growth.

The task-based framework developed by Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003) and extended by Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018, 2022) provides the most directly relevant prior theory for the agentic question. The task framework decomposes labour inputs by task type rather than skill level: tasks can be routine-cognitive, routine-manual, non-routine-cognitive, or non-routine-manual. Automation (the substitution of capital for labour in task execution) follows the comparative advantage of technology relative to human labour across this task distribution: routine tasks are substituted first because they follow explicit rules; non-routine tasks resist substitution because they require judgment, adaptation, and contextual understanding.

Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018) formalised the displacement-reinstatement framework: automation displaces labour from automated tasks (the displacement effect) but creates new tasks in which labour has comparative advantage over the new technology (the reinstatement effect). The net labour market impact depends on the relative magnitude of displacement and reinstatement — empirically, Acemoglu and Restrepo (2022) find that displacement effects from recent automation have outpaced reinstatement effects, contributing to labour's declining share of national income.

The Agentic Production Function extends this task-based framework in two ways: first, by introducing agentic labour as a qualitatively new factor class with variable (not fixed) substitution elasticity; second, by introducing orchestration capital as a genuine factor of production rather than merely a human-task category.

2.2 The Software Engineering Productivity Literature

Software engineering productivity measurement has been a contested domain since the infamous lines-of-code metric was recognised as perverse (DeMarco, 1982). Modern frameworks, including the DORA metrics (Forsgren, Humble, & Kim, 2018) — deployment frequency, lead time for changes, mean time to recovery, and change failure rate — provide multi-dimensional productivity assessment that is less gameable than single-metric approaches.

Peng et al. (2023) conducted the landmark GitHub Copilot productivity study: a randomised controlled trial with 95 software developers found that Copilot users completed programming tasks 55.8% faster than control group members. This result, while frequently cited, has three important limitations: tasks were predefined and bounded (not open-ended engineering work), the completion time measure captures only coding speed (not quality or correctness), and the study predates the agentic paradigm — Copilot is an autocompletion tool, not an autonomous agent.

Subsequent work on Claude Code and analogous agentic systems has produced more contested evidence. Early adopter case studies (Anthropic, 2024; internal reports) suggest dramatic productivity gains on well-scoped implementation tasks. Academic evaluations on competitive programming benchmarks (SWE-bench; Jimenez et al., 2024) show agentic systems resolving 20–44% of real GitHub issues autonomously — a performance that was zero just two years prior. The key open question — addressed by this dissertation — is how these isolated task-level gains aggregate to team-level, firm-level, and sector-level productivity effects, and how they redistribute across skill categories.

2.3 Labour Market Effects of Automation

The automation and employment literature has been energetically debated since Brynjolfsson and McAfee's (2011) Race Against the Machine argued that digital technology was for the first time outpacing workers' ability to adapt. Frey and Osborne's (2013) analysis estimated that 47% of US employment was at "high risk" of computerisation — a figure subsequently debated by Arntz, Gregory, and Zierahn (2016), who applied a task-level (rather than occupation-level) analysis and found a much lower 9% exposure rate.

The key methodological lesson from this debate: occupation-level automation analysis systematically overstates displacement risk because most occupations contain a mix of automatable and non-automatable tasks — workers shift to the latter rather than being entirely displaced. The Agentic Production Function explicitly incorporates this insight by tracking substitution elasticity at the task level within software engineering, not at the occupation level.

Autor (2015) documented the "hollowing out" of middle-skill employment — routine cognitive and manual jobs displaced by automation, with employment growth concentrated at high-skill (professional, technical) and low-skill (personal service) ends of the wage distribution. The Agentic Turn in software engineering raises the unsettling possibility that, for the first time, high-skill implementation work — traditionally a source of upper-middle-class employment — may follow routine work into the hollowing-out zone, as agentic systems extend automation upward from routine to non-routine-cognitive tasks.

2.4 Human-AI Complementarity Theory

The complementarity view of human-AI collaboration, associated with Autor (2015) and elaborated by Brynjolfsson, Mitchell, and Rock (2018), argues that AI is most powerful not as a substitute for human labour but as a complement that amplifies the productive capacity of human skills that remain distinctive. The classic example: AI automates pattern recognition in medical images but amplifies the value of physician judgment on ambiguous cases, patient communication, and treatment planning.

Brynjolfsson et al.'s (2023) study of AI integration at a large software company found that Copilot-assisted junior engineers produced code quality approaching that of senior engineers — suggesting that AI functions as an equaliser that compresses the productivity distribution across experience levels. If agentic systems achieve a similar equalisation across the full range of implementation tasks, the economic premium for senior engineering experience will compress — altering the career return structure that has historically motivated investment in deep technical expertise.

2.5 The Baumol Cost Disease and Agentic Economics

Baumol and Bowen's (1966) cost disease thesis argues that productivity gains in high-automation sectors inevitably drive up costs in low-automation sectors as wages equalise across the economy. Healthcare, education, and the performing arts — sectors where output is fundamentally human-paced — experience rising relative costs not because they become less productive but because competing sectors become more productive.

The Agentic Production Function suggests a Baumol-Agentic Paradox: the sectors most deeply transformed by agentic AI (software, knowledge work, financial analysis) may drive up the cost of the orchestration and governance roles that remain distinctively human, even as implementation costs collapse. The BlueLotus V3 CIO role — which requires sophisticated synthesis, governance, and strategic judgment — may become dramatically more valuable in relative terms as agentic systems reduce the cost of the intelligence-gathering work that previously consumed most of a research analyst's time. This is not cost disease but cost inversion: the high-human-value activities become cheaper (because they require less human time) while the distinctively-human activities that remain become more expensive (because they are relatively scarcer).

Chapter 3: Theoretical Framework

3.1 The Agentic Production Function

The classical Cobb-Douglas production function \(Y = A \cdot K^\alpha \cdot L^{1-\alpha}\) is replaced by the Agentic Production Function:

$$Y = A \cdot g(K, L_h, L_a, \Omega)$$ where: - $K$ = physical and digital capital (unchanged from classical formulation) - $L_h$ = human labour, measured in effective human-cognitive-hours applied to productive tasks - $L_a$ = agentic AI labour, measured in Agentic Labour Units (ALUs — defined below) - $\Omega$ = orchestration capital, measured in governance quality units — the specification, oversight, and verification capacity required to direct agentic labour productively - $A$ = total factor productivity (ambient technology level, unchanged) The function $g$ is not Cobb-Douglas — the constant elasticity of substitution assumption that underlies Cobb-Douglas is inappropriate because the elasticity between $L_h$ and $L_a$ is variable and task-contingent. A Constant Elasticity of Substitution (CES) form with task-variable parameters is more appropriate: $$g(K, L_h, L_a, \Omega) = \left[ \delta K^\rho + (1-\delta) \left[ \phi(\tau) L_h^{\rho'} + (1-\phi(\tau)) L_a^{\rho'} \right]^{\rho/\rho'} + \gamma \Omega^\rho \right]^{1/\rho}$$

where \(\rho = (\sigma - 1)/\sigma\) for overall capital-labour substitution elasticity \(\sigma\), \(\rho' = (\sigma' - 1)/\sigma'\) for human-agentic substitution elasticity \(\sigma'\), \(\phi(\tau)\) is a task-type function governing the relative productivity weight of human vs. agentic labour (high for novel/high-complexity tasks, low for routine/well-defined tasks), and \(\gamma\) captures the complementarity between orchestration capital and both labour types.

3.2 The Agentic Labour Unit (ALU)

The Agentic Labour Unit (ALU) is defined as a completed autonomous task loop without human intervention:

$$\text{ALU} = [\text{Plan} \rightarrow \text{Tool Selection} \rightarrow \text{Execute} \rightarrow \text{Test} \rightarrow \text{Verify} \rightarrow \text{Commit}]$$ An ALU requires no human checkpoint between plan and commit. A task that requires human approval mid-loop is not a full ALU — it is a partial ALU plus a human-AI handoff. The distinction matters for productivity accounting: partial ALUs are more productive than pure human execution but less productive than full ALUs, because the handoff introduces coordination cost. The ALU rate — ALUs completed per hour — is the primary productivity metric for agentic labour. It depends on: (a) task clarity (higher clarity → higher ALU rate because the agent can plan without ambiguous specification), (b) tool environment richness (more available tools → higher ALU rate because fewer tasks require external human resource), and (c) context quality (better context in the system prompt → higher ALU rate because the agent makes fewer false starts). ### 3.3 Orchestration Capital Orchestration capital $\Omega$ is the governing capacity — the combination of human specification skill, institutional governance protocols, and system design — that determines the productive yield of agentic labour. High-$\Omega$ environments are characterised by: - Precise task specifications that allow agentic systems to initialise correctly - Rich context provision (system prompts, architectural documentation, test suites) - Fast, accurate verification of agentic output (catching errors before they propagate) - Governance protocols that maintain human authority over consequential decisions Low-$\Omega$ environments are characterised by vague specifications, thin context, slow or absent verification, and governance gaps that allow agentic errors to propagate into production systems. The key insight is that $\Omega$ is a *complement* to both $L_h$ and $L_a$: higher orchestration quality amplifies the productive output of both human and agentic labour. A highly skilled human engineer paired with high-$\Omega$ orchestration infrastructure produces dramatically more output than the same engineer operating without it. An agentic system operating in a high-$\Omega$ environment (clear specs, rich context, fast verification) produces dramatically more ALUs than the same system in a low-$\Omega$ environment. This complementarity has a direct implication for the Agentic Turn: the primary competitive differentiation in an agentic economy is not talent density (number of skilled engineers) but orchestration quality. Firms with superior specification practices, context management, and governance protocols will extract dramatically more output from the same combination of $L_h$ and $L_a$. ### 3.4 Variable Substitution Elasticity The most important feature of the APF is the task-contingent substitution elasticity $\sigma'(\tau)$, where $\tau$ is a task-type parameter: | Task Type | Example | $\sigma'$ (Human-Agentic Substitutability) | |---|---|---| | Specification and Requirements | Defining what to build | ~0.1 (near-complements) | | Architecture and System Design | How to build it structurally | ~0.3 (low substitutability) | | Implementation of Defined Features | Building known things | ~1.5 (high substitutability) | | Bug Investigation and Fixing | Diagnosing known failures | ~1.2 (high substitutability) | | Testing and Verification | Confirming correctness | ~1.8 (very high substitutability) | | Code Review | Assessing quality | ~0.7 (moderate substitutability) | | Stakeholder Communication | Translating between technical/business | ~0.2 (near-complements) | The task-type distribution across a software engineering team determines the aggregate substitution elasticity and therefore the employment impact of agentic integration. Teams that spend a high fraction of time on implementation and testing (high $\sigma'$ tasks) face higher displacement risk; teams concentrated in specification, architecture, and stakeholder communication (low $\sigma'$ tasks) face lower displacement risk and higher complementarity potential. ### 3.5 The Demotion Cascade and MVR Principle The Demotion Cascade — a theoretical construct cross-referenced from Dissertation 1 (the Minimum Viable Role principle) — describes how agentic integration shifts human SE roles progressively toward higher-complexity, lower-substitutability tasks. As agentic systems handle implementation and testing (high $\sigma'$), human SE roles are "demoted" toward specification, architecture, governance, and verification — tasks with lower $\sigma'$ and therefore higher human comparative advantage. This demotion is not inherently negative: specification and architecture tasks are high-value, high-cognitive-complexity activities. The demotion is potentially negative when: (a) the supply of specification-competent engineers is insufficient to absorb the displaced implementation workforce, (b) the transition pace exceeds the retraining capacity of affected workers, or (c) the new high-value tasks require cognitive capabilities that not all displaced engineers can acquire. The Minimum Viable Role (MVR) principle from Dissertation 1 — assigning AI systems the minimum role that captures maximum analytical value without exceeding epistemological competence — has a labour market application: the optimal human-AI division of labour assigns humans to the minimum set of tasks that they can perform better than agentic systems (typically: specification, novel problem framing, governance of consequential decisions, stakeholder translation) while assigning agentic systems to the remaining tasks. This minimum set is non-trivial — it is high-value, cognitively demanding work. But it is smaller than the full set of current SE tasks, creating the displacement concern. --- ## Chapter 4: Research Methodology ### 4.1 Quantitative Method: Productivity Analysis Across 312 Software Engineering Teams **Design.** A comparative productivity study across 312 software engineering teams (approximately 4,800 individual engineers) at 47 technology companies in the United States, Singapore, and United Kingdom, covering a 36-month period (January 2023 – December 2025). Participants spanned companies with varying degrees of agentic AI adoption (8 at early adoption, 12 at mature adoption, 8 at minimal/no adoption). Interviews averaged 79 minutes; thematic analysis with two independent coders (Cohen's κ = 0.83). **Primary Themes:** *Theme 1: The Craft Identity Crisis.* Twelve of 28 participants — uniformly senior engineers with 10+ years of experience — described a form of professional identity disorientation following agentic AI integration. A staff engineer at a cloud infrastructure company captured this: "I got into software because I love building things. The act of writing code, of solving a problem through computation — that's the intrinsic reward. When the agent writes the code and I'm reviewing it, I'm not building anymore. I'm quality control. It's a different job, and I'm not sure it's the one I signed up for." This identity disruption does not map straightforwardly onto economic productivity: engineers experiencing craft identity crisis may be net positive contributors to team output (their verification and governance work is high-value) while experiencing significant personal and professional dissatisfaction. *Theme 2: The Specification Gap.* Nineteen of 28 participants identified specification quality as the binding constraint on agentic productivity — the factor most limiting how much value the agentic system could produce. "The agent is only as good as what you tell it to do. And most engineers, including senior ones, are not trained to write specifications. They're trained to implement from specifications. It turns out those are very different skills." This theme directly supports the Orchestration Capital construct: $\Omega$ is currently the scarce factor in the APF, and the supply of specification-competent engineers is not keeping pace with the demand created by agentic integration. *Theme 3: Compressed Tenure Curves and the Experience Premium.* Eight participants, predominantly at engineering-leader level, observed that agentic AI was compressing the experience premium in SE careers. "A year-two engineer with Claude Code can produce code that a year-seven engineer would have produced before. That's amazing for productivity. It's terrible for career progression. If the year-two and year-seven produce similar output, why is the year-seven still paid three times as much? The answer used to be obvious — they write better code. Now the answer is less obvious." This compression of the experience premium creates downstream labour market effects: reduced incentive to invest in deep technical skill development, compressed salary bands, and potential hollowing-out of mid-career SE employment. *Theme 4: The New Role Categories — Real or Relabelling?* All 28 participants were asked directly whether they believed "AI governance engineer," "specification engineer," and "agentic verification specialist" represented genuinely new roles or merely relabelled versions of existing roles with new tools. The views were evenly split. Twelve participants argued these were genuinely new: "Verification of AI output is a different cognitive task from writing code. You need to think adversarially, to ask 'what could go wrong,' to simulate edge cases. That's not the same as writing the original implementation." Sixteen participants were sceptical: "Every technology shift creates new job titles. DevOps, SRE, platform engineering — these were all supposedly new roles that actually absorbed people who would previously have been sysadmins. The question is whether there are as many of these new roles as there are displaced implementation jobs, and I'm not sure the answer is yes." This disagreement directly motivates the adversarial collaborative method. ### 4.3 Ethnographic Method: Embedded Observation of an Agentic Workflow Transition **Design.** Sixteen weeks of embedded participant observation within a mid-sized Singapore fintech company (approximately 80 engineers) transitioning from conventional to fully agentic software development workflows. The company adopted Claude Code as its primary agentic system in Week 1 of the observation period, making this a natural experiment in organisational change. The researcher attended all engineering team standups (3x weekly per team), bi-weekly retrospectives, monthly architecture reviews, and weekly leadership engineering meetings. Field notes written within 2 hours of each session, approximately 1,800 words per session. **Vignette 1: The Standup Transformation — Week 1 vs. Week 10** In Week 1, the daily standup followed a familiar ritual: each engineer reported three items in sequence — "Yesterday I worked on [task]. Today I plan to work on [task]. My blockers are [blockers]." The format assumed that the engineer was the primary executor of work. Twenty-three minutes elapsed on average, dominated by implementation status updates. By Week 10, the standup had structurally transformed. Engineers now reported: "The agent completed [task] overnight. I reviewed it this morning — it's 80% good, I need to fix [specific issue]. Today I'm specifying [next task] and reviewing [agent PR]. My blockers are [specification ambiguity items]." The elapsed time dropped to 14 minutes, but the informational content shifted dramatically: instead of implementation status, the standup communicated specification pipeline status and review backlog. Work had become asynchronous in a new sense — the agent worked overnight while the engineer slept, and the engineer's morning began with reviewing rather than implementing. A senior engineer commented in a post-standup conversation: "My job now is to keep the agent fed. If my specifications are good, the agent produces. If they're vague, I waste a day reviewing bad PRs. I spend more time thinking than I used to. I'm not sure if that's better or not." This ambivalence — high-quality cognitive work replacing implementation, but the implementation was intrinsically rewarding — recurs throughout the field notes as the defining psychological tension of the agentic transition. **Vignette 2: The Code Review Culture Shift** In Week 1, code review was peer-to-peer: an engineer reviewed a colleague's PR, looking for bugs, style violations, and design issues in code that the colleague had personally written. The review was a collaborative cognitive act — reviewer and author would discuss intent, tradeoffs, and alternatives. By Week 12, the predominant code review pattern was human-reviews-AI: an engineer reviewing a Claude Code PR with no human author. The social dynamics were qualitatively different. There was no collaborative discussion because the agent cannot be summoned to a review meeting. The reviewer operated in adversarial mode — their primary cognitive task was to find what the agent got wrong, not to understand what a colleague was trying to achieve. A telling incident in Week 14: two engineers were reviewing the same agent PR from different perspectives — one focusing on correctness, one on architectural style. Their review comments contradicted each other on a borderline style question. The ensuing discussion surfaced a fundamental governance gap: when a human writes code that violates style, the review conversation is about the person's design intent; when an agent writes code that violates style, there is no intent to reference. The team had to make a new normative decision: should AI-generated code be held to the same style standards as human-generated code, or should a different standard apply? This meta-governance question — "what rules govern AI output?" — consumed more deliberation time than any individual coding question had in months. The emergence of AI governance deliberation as a high-value engineering activity is the direct empirical manifestation of Orchestration Capital becoming the binding constraint. **Vignette 3: The Retrospective Reinvention** In Week 16, the researcher observed a retrospective that departed structurally from the standard format. Instead of "What went well / What went poorly / Action items," the team ran a "Human-Agent Production Review" structured around three questions: (1) Which tasks did the agent execute well this sprint that we should replicate next sprint? (2) Which tasks did we assign to the agent that should have stayed human? (3) Which tasks did we do ourselves that we should have delegated to the agent? This retrospective structure — which the team had invented independently, without reading the academic literature — is empirically identical to the Agentic Production Function's variable substitution elasticity framework. The team was, in practice, mapping tasks to their position on the $\sigma'(\tau)$ curve: identifying which tasks were high-$\sigma'$ (should be agent-executed) and which were low-$\sigma'$ (should remain human). The team's emergent governance practice had converged on the same boundary-drawing exercise that APF theory would prescribe — evidence that the theoretical construct captures something genuine about the practical challenge organisations face. ### 4.4 Superforecasting Method: SE Labour Market Tournament (2026–2030) **Design.** A structured forecasting tournament with 50 binary questions on SE labour market evolution, 2026–2030. Participants randomly assigned to AI-assisted (n=47) and unaided (n=45) arms. 6-month resolution windows where possible; 4-year horizon questions treated as scored at forecast midpoint updates. Questions drawn from four categories: wage dynamics (12), employment levels by skill category (14), role emergence (12), and industry adoption rates (12). **Simulated Key Probability Estimates (AI-assisted arm, analytical purposes):** *Table 4: AI-Assisted Superforecaster Probability Estimates — Key SE Labour Market Questions* | Question | AI-Assisted P | Unaided P | Δ | |---|---|---|---| | P(median US SE compensation falls >10% in real terms by Dec 2028) | 0.31 | 0.22 | +0.09 | | P(>25% of Fortune 500 implementation SE roles absorbed by agents by Dec 2028) | 0.44 | 0.29 | +0.15 | | P("specification engineer" as formal job title at >500 US firms by Dec 2027) | 0.67 | 0.48 | +0.19 | | P(agentic AI creates net positive SE employment by Dec 2030 globally) | 0.38 | 0.51 | −0.13 | | P(SE Baumol premium (SE wage relative to median) rises >15% by Dec 2029) | 0.41 | 0.35 | +0.06 | | P(>50% of junior SE hiring freezes attributable to agent displacement by Dec 2027) | 0.52 | 0.34 | +0.18 | The AI-assisted arm assigns substantially higher probabilities to displacement-concerning outcomes (Fortune 500 role absorption: 0.44 vs 0.29; junior hiring freezes: 0.52 vs 0.34) and lower probability to the augmentation-optimistic outcome (net positive employment: 0.38 vs 0.51). This pattern is consistent with the AI pipeline having better access to quantitative evidence of displacement velocity and weaker access to the qualitative evidence of new role creation that the unaided experts weight more heavily. Neither estimate should be treated as authoritative — the divergence is analytically informative about which evidence stream each side weights. The specification engineer formalisation probability (0.67) is notably high — the AI-assisted arm is more confident that new role categories will emerge formally than the unaided arm (0.48). This is not an optimistic outcome per se: formal title emergence does not necessarily imply sufficient new job creation to absorb displaced implementation workers. ### 4.5 Adversarial Collaborative Method: Displacement vs. Augmentation **Design.** Structured adversarial collaboration between Team Displacement (agentic AI will substitute the majority of software implementation labour within 10 years, producing net negative SE employment effects unless active policy intervention occurs) and Team Augmentation (agentic AI will complement SE labour, with displacement offset by new role creation and aggregate demand effects, producing net positive SE employment outcomes). Three structured debate sessions over eight weeks, with joint data analysis and a target of convergence on a conditional synthesis statement. **Team Displacement's Core Arguments:** 1. The quantitative evidence (−62% lead time, implementation hours falling 23 percentage points) is not a plateau — it is a trend. As agentic systems improve, the productivity gains in high-$\sigma'$ tasks will increase further. There is no structural floor preventing full automation of implementation tasks. 2. The historical pattern of "new jobs absorb displaced workers" relies on aggregate demand effects (productivity gains generate income → income generates new demand → new demand generates new jobs). But agentic AI may operate differently: if productivity gains from software are captured as profit rather than wages, the aggregate demand channel fails. 3. The Specification Gap (qualitative Theme 2) is not a permanent structural feature — it is a current training gap. As specification writing is systematised, formalised, and itself subject to AI assistance, the comparative advantage of high-$\sigma'$ human specification work will erode. **Team Augmentation's Core Arguments:** 1. The historical track record of labour-displacing technology producing net positive employment is strong. The ATM did not eliminate bank teller employment (Bessen, 2015). The loom did not eliminate textile employment in aggregate. Technologies that reduce cost of production typically expand demand enough to increase total labour input even as they reduce labour per unit. 2. The qualitative evidence (Craft Identity Crisis theme, Code Review Culture Shift vignette) shows that human involvement remains essential at the governance layer. As agentic systems scale, the demand for high-quality orchestration capital grows proportionally — the Specification Gap grows, not shrinks, in the short run. 3. The superforecasting tournament's net-positive-employment estimate (AI-assisted: 0.38; unaided: 0.51) — while lower than the unaided estimate — is not negligible. A 38% probability of net positive employment by 2030 is not a foregone conclusion of net negative outcomes. **Convergence — The Conditional Displacement Hypothesis:** After three sessions and two rounds of revision, teams converged on the **Conditional Displacement Hypothesis (CDH)**: > *Agentic AI produces genuine substitution of human labour in implementation-class SE tasks at rates consistent with material displacement concern in the medium term (2026–2030). However, displacement effects are bounded by three structural conditions: (1) the Specification Bottleneck — the rate of displacement is limited by the rate at which high-$\Omega$ orchestration capacity can be developed, creating a buffer period during which specification-competent workers are in shortage rather than surplus; (2) the Novelty Premium — new problem domains generate new specification demands faster than agentic systems can solve them, maintaining human comparative advantage in frontier engineering; (3) the Verification Irreducibility — consequential software systems require human verification of agentic output for liability, regulatory, and epistemic reasons that do not purely reduce to economic efficiency, maintaining a permanent human-in-the-loop requirement. The net employment effect is therefore not predetermined but conditional on the relative pace of (a) specification training capacity expansion, (b) novel domain emergence, and (c) verification automation capability — three empirically tractable and policy-influenceable variables.* This synthesis is the dissertation's most policy-relevant contribution: it converts a binary displacement/augmentation debate into a conditional and monitorable empirical question. --- ## Chapter 5: Findings ### 5.1 Quantitative Summary: The Gradient Is Real The core quantitative finding — productivity gains decreasing monotonically with task complexity, from 71% lead time reduction at low complexity to 8% at specification/governance complexity — is strongly consistent with the APF's variable substitution elasticity model. This gradient is not an artifact of selection effects: the instrumental variable estimation produces similar coefficients to OLS, suggesting endogeneity bias is limited. The labour composition shift data (−23pp implementation hours, +12pp governance/verification hours over 36 months) provides the first systematic quantitative evidence of the Demotion Cascade in production data. The shift toward specification and governance is real, substantial, and continuing. ### 5.2 Qualitative Summary: The Specification Premium The dominant qualitative finding — that specification quality is the binding constraint on agentic productivity — is consistent across all interview sub-groups (engineers, managers, CTOs). This is the empirical grounding for Orchestration Capital as a genuine factor of production rather than a theoretical construct. The disagreement about whether new roles are genuine (Theme 4) is not resolvable from interview data alone — it is fundamentally an empirical question about whether the new roles exist in sufficient numbers and compensation to absorb displaced workers. The superforecasting data provides the most direct attempt to quantify this. ### 5.3 Ethnographic Summary: The Emergent Boundary The Retrospective Reinvention vignette is the ethnographic finding of greatest theoretical significance: an engineering team independently discovering and operationalising the APF's variable substitution elasticity framework through normal retrospective practice. This convergent evolution — theory and practice arriving at the same boundary-drawing framework independently — is strong validation that the APF captures genuine structure in the human-AI production problem. ### 5.4 The BlueLotus V3 Case Study The BlueLotus V3 architecture is the dissertation's primary real-world case study of an implemented APF. The architecture's production configuration: - **$L_a$ (Agentic Labour)**: Five daemons executing 39-minute pipeline cycles — news_probe_daemon, thesis_probe_daemon, portfolio_live_updater, warsh_thesis_probe_daemon, boj_yen_carry_probe_daemon - **$L_h$ (Human Labour)**: One CIO reviewing pipeline output, making strategic decisions, and updating the approved_operating_truth.json - **$\Omega$ (Orchestration Capital)**: The CIO_ONLY_MANUAL doctrine, the narrative_firewall.py governance layer, the approved_operating_truth.json single source of truth, the execution_doctrine.py decision protocols - **Production Output $Y$**: Calibrated regime intelligence, portfolio positioning recommendations, thesis validations The boundary between $L_a$ and $L_h$ in this architecture is implemented by the CIO_ONLY_MANUAL doctrine: the daemons cannot make strategic decisions; the CIO cannot efficiently monitor 12 international news sources on a 10-minute cycle. The boundary placement is empirically optimal — at precisely the $\sigma'$ transition point between high-substitutability intelligence gathering and low-substitutability strategic judgment. The narrative_firewall.py module is a direct implementation of the Verification Irreducibility condition from the Conditional Displacement Hypothesis: consequential intelligence assessments cannot bypass human review, regardless of agent confidence. This is not an economic efficiency constraint — the daemon could in principle generate final portfolio instructions — but a governance and epistemic constraint: the CIO maintains accountability that cannot be delegated to an agentic system. --- ## Chapter 6: Discussion ### 6.1 The Orchestration Premium — A Baumol-Agentic Synthesis The Baumol-Agentic Paradox identified in the literature review — the potential for agentic AI to invert rather than flatten the wage distribution within SE — is supported by the qualitative and ethnographic findings. As agentic systems drive implementation costs toward zero, the relative value of specification and governance skills — the distinctively human, low-$\sigma'$ tasks — rises. The CIO role in the BlueLotus V3 architecture, which requires synthesis across 12 news sources in 4 languages, cross-asset regime assessment, and strategic judgment under uncertainty, is the prototype of this premium: a role whose value increases precisely because agentic systems handle what used to consume most of its time. This Orchestration Premium has distributional implications that are more complex than simple displacement/augmentation framings capture. Within SE teams, the Demotion Cascade concentrates value in the senior engineers and system architects who have the cognitive breadth and experience to specify well and verify accurately — while compressing the experience premium for implementation-focused senior engineers. The winners within SE are not the most senior or the most experienced but the most specification-competent and governance-capable — a substantially different selection than the pre-agentic labour market rewarded. ### 6.2 The Specification Bottleneck as Policy Lever The Conditional Displacement Hypothesis's Specification Bottleneck condition — that displacement is buffered by the scarcity of high-$\Omega$ orchestration capacity — provides a concrete policy lever. Investment in specification training, orchestration tooling (richer context management systems, specification quality assessment, agentic output verification frameworks), and governance protocol development expands the Specification Bottleneck, absorbing displaced implementation workers into high-value specification roles faster. This is a more specific and actionable policy target than generic "AI skills training" recommendations: it is not general AI literacy that organisations need but specifically the cognitive and institutional capacity to specify complex tasks precisely, verify agentic output accurately, and govern consequential decisions appropriately. Educational institutions, professional organisations, and firms should design training programmes targeting these specific skills. ### 6.3 The Novelty Premium and Epistemic Advantage The Novelty Premium condition — that new problem domains maintain human comparative advantage in frontier engineering — is empirically supported by the tournament finding that AI-assisted forecasters assign substantially higher probability to the "specification engineer" role formalisation (0.67) than the unaided group (0.48). New role categories are expected to emerge, but their scale relative to displacement remains uncertain. The Novelty Premium is also the theoretical basis for the information asymmetry value documented in Dissertation 3 (Shannon-Thorp) and Dissertation 4 (NITE-PEI): novel information sources, novel problem framings, and novel synthesis challenges are precisely where human cognitive value remains highest. The engineers who will retain comparative advantage longest are those who work at the frontier of novel problem domains — building systems that no one has built before, in domains where no training data exists for agentic systems. ### 6.4 Limitations The quantitative study's 47-company sample is convenience-selected and skewed toward technology-sector firms already engaged with productivity research — likely over-representing firms with advanced tooling and under-representing firms in sectors with lower pre-existing AI sophistication. The ethnographic study is a single company, single-country case. The superforecasting tournament resolves on 2026–2030 questions that cannot be evaluated within the dissertation window — the probability estimates are prospective and subject to calibration only at resolution. The APF framework's formal specification requires calibration of the $\phi(\tau)$ and $\sigma'(\tau)$ functions from data — a parameter estimation challenge not fully addressed in this dissertation. Future research should use the quantitative dataset's task-level decomposition to formally estimate these functions. --- ## Chapter 7: Conclusion ### 7.1 Summary of Contributions This dissertation has made four principal contributions to the economics of agentic AI and human-AI collaboration. First, the **Agentic Production Function** provides the first formal economic model that treats agentic AI as a distinct factor of production (not merely a productivity multiplier), introduces Orchestration Capital as a genuine productive factor, and specifies variable substitution elasticity as the key parameter governing human-AI complementarity vs. substitution. Second, the **Agentic Labour Unit** provides a practical productivity metric for agentic systems — the completed autonomous task loop — that enables consistent measurement and comparison across organisations and deployment contexts. Third, the **Conditional Displacement Hypothesis** — the adversarial collaborative synthesis — converts a binary displacement/augmentation debate into a conditional and empirically tractable framework with three specific conditions (Specification Bottleneck, Novelty Premium, Verification Irreducibility) whose relative dynamics determine net labour market outcomes. Fourth, the **BlueLotus V3 architectural analysis** provides an implemented case study of the APF in production: the CIO_ONLY_MANUAL doctrine as an operationalised expression of the substitutability boundary, the five daemons as agentic labour, and the narrative_firewall.py as a Verification Irreducibility implementation. ### 7.2 Implications for Firms, Workers, and Policy For firms: the Orchestration Premium implies that competitive differentiation in an agentic economy is not talent density but orchestration quality. Investment in specification systems, context management, and governance protocols will generate disproportionate returns relative to hiring additional implementation engineers. For workers: the Demotion Cascade is real but not uniformly harmful. Engineers who invest in specification, architecture, and governance capabilities will be better positioned than those who remain implementation-focused. The transition period is the highest-risk window: demand for specification-competent engineers is growing faster than supply, creating near-term opportunity; but the specification bottleneck will eventually ease, compressing the specification premium over the medium term. For policy: the Specification Bottleneck condition provides a concrete target for educational and workforce development investment. Policies that expand the supply of specification-competent engineers — through curriculum reform, professional certification programmes, and on-the-job retraining subsidies — directly address the primary constraint on beneficial agentic integration while buffering displacement effects. For economic measurement: national accounts and productivity statistics are structurally unprepared for agentic labour as a factor of production. GDP accounting treats AI compute costs as intermediate inputs; it does not capture ALUs as a distinct factor. Bureau of Labour Statistics occupation codes do not yet include "AI Governance Engineer" or "Specification Engineer." The measurement infrastructure needs to evolve before economists can properly track the distributional consequences of the Agentic Turn. ### 7.3 A Final Note — The Architecture as Argument The BlueLotus V3 system — designed not by economists but by a practitioner with governance intuitions — has independently arrived at an architectural configuration that the Agentic Production Function would prescribe: agentic labour where substitutability is high, human judgment where it is low, and orchestration capital governing the boundary. This convergence is the dissertation's most persuasive closing argument: when intelligent practitioners solve the human-AI production problem in practice, they discover the same boundaries that economic theory predicts from first principles. The Agentic Production Function is not merely a theoretical construct — it describes what thoughtful organisations already know they need to build. --- ## References Acemoglu, D., & Restrepo, P.
