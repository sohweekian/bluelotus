Why Ghana Matters — Sub-Saharan Debt Stress Context

The IMF Case Study of the 2020s

Ghana defaulted on its external debt in December 2022 — the first Sub-Saharan African country to do so since Zambia in 2020. Ghana's Domestic Debt Exchange Programme (DDEP) restructured GHS 82 billion of domestic bonds in 2023, imposing significant haircuts on local pension funds and banks. The IMF programme was approved April 2023 — $3B over 4 years — making Ghana the largest Sub-Saharan IMF programme of the 2020s.

Dual Commodity Exposure — Unique in Africa

Ghana is simultaneously the world's 2nd largest cocoa producer (15% of global supply; COCOBOD state board) and Africa's largest gold producer (115 tonnes/year; 35% of export revenues). Cocoa prices hit a record $10,000+/tonne in 2024. This dual commodity dependency means Ghana's fiscal position is uniquely exposed to two uncorrelated commodity shocks simultaneously.

Contagion Risk: Ghana's debt restructuring serves as the template for Sub-Saharan EM debt stress management. A programme breach would signal to markets that the Zambia/Ghana restructuring model has failed, potentially triggering EM debt risk-off across Kenya, Nigeria, Ethiopia, and other frontier-market borrowers.
