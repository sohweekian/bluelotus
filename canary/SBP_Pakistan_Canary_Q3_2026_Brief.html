Rate Path: Emergency Tightening → Easing Cycle

The SBP's monetary policy history from 2022 to 2026 represents one of the most dramatic tightening-and-easing cycles in emerging market central banking. Beginning from a policy rate of 7.0% in early 2022, the SBP was forced to raise rates to 22.0% by June 2023 — one of the highest policy rates of any major central bank globally — as Pakistan's triple crisis (balance of payments, FX reserves, and inflation) reached its apex. The IMF bailout preconditions required energy price deregulation and fiscal tightening, which pushed headline CPI to a record 38.0% in May 2023.

The easing cycle began in H2 2024 as IMF targets were met, FX reserves recovered, and inflation began its structural decline. By Q3 2026, the policy rate has been cut to approximately 13%, reflecting continued disinflation but still-elevated real rates given lingering inflation. The easing pace is constrained by IMF structural benchmarks requiring SBP operational independence and fiscal discipline.

FX Reserves — The Crisis and Recovery

Pakistan's FX reserves at the State Bank hit a critical low of approximately $3.0 billion in January 2023 — covering less than 3 weeks of imports. This was the proximate trigger for Pakistan's 22nd IMF visit. The subsequent $3B Stand-By Arrangement (SBA), followed by IMF approval of a $7B Extended Fund Facility (EFF) in July 2024, unlocked multilateral and bilateral creditor flows. By Q3 2026, SBP reserves have recovered to approximately $8-9B, providing approximately 2 months of import cover — still below the 3-month threshold considered adequate for EM sovereigns.

Pakistan and the IMF: A 68-Year Relationship

Pakistan has entered into 20 IMF programmes since its first in 1958 — more than any other country in history. This frequency reflects structural fiscal imbalances: chronic current account deficits, a narrow tax base (~9% of GDP), energy sector circular debt, and military expenditure pressures. Only a fraction of programmes have achieved structural reform goals; most have been followed within 3-5 years by a return to the IMF.

The current $7B EFF (July 2024) is Pakistan's largest ever IMF facility. Key structural benchmarks include: SBP operational independence (no direct SBP financing of fiscal deficit), energy tariff adjustments to reduce circular debt, revenue mobilisation (FBR target enforcement), and SOE reform milestones. KC_02 score of 0.55 reflects the programme being on track but fragile — Pakistan's historical completion rate is low, and political pressures create quarterly cliff-edges at each IMF review.

A confirmed IMF programme suspension or non-completion of a quarterly review would trigger a balance-of-payments crisis: FX reserves would rapidly drain, USDPKR would spike, and KC_03 and KC_02 would both fire into ELEVATED territory, driving PKRI towards the 0.65-0.85 ELEVATED zone.

January 2023: The Reserve Crisis

Pakistan's forex reserves at the SBP hit a critical nadir of approximately $3.0 billion in January 2023 — a level that triggered near-default conditions. At that level, Pakistan's import cover was less than 3 weeks. The government imposed import restrictions, rationed FX allocations, and faced debt service pressures on Eurobonds and bilateral creditors simultaneously. The IMF SBA negotiation was the critical lifeline.

The recovery path from $3.0B to $8-9B reflects: $3B IMF SBA disbursements, Saudi Arabia and UAE bilateral deposits (~$3B combined), World Bank and ADB project loans, and partial normalization of remittances (Pakistan's diaspora remittances of ~$27B annually are a critical FX source). CPEC infrastructure payments from China also contribute to the reserve stabilisation, though Chinese debt service creates an offsetting outflow.

KC_03 score of 0.60 — the highest kill condition score in the PKRI stack — reflects that while recovery is underway, Pakistan remains one shock away from reserve stress: an IMF suspension, a commodity price spike, or a geopolitical event could rapidly reverse the recovery trajectory.

The Pakistan Hyperinflation Episode (2022-2023)

Pakistan's inflation crisis of 2022-2023 was driven by a combination of global commodity shocks (post-Ukraine energy prices), PKR devaluation (each 10% PKR depreciation added approximately 2-3pp to CPI via import costs), energy tariff increases mandated by the IMF as a precondition, and supply chain disruptions from the catastrophic 2022 floods (affecting approximately one-third of Pakistan's territory).

CPI reaching 38.0% in May 2023 placed Pakistan among the most inflation-afflicted economies globally at that time — comparable to emerging market stress cases like Turkey and Argentina. The SBP's emergency rate hikes to 22% were necessary but caused significant economic pain, with credit growth collapsing and private investment stalling.

KC_04 score of 0.55 reflects the declining trajectory (assessed positive delta) but persistent above-target inflation. The downside risk: energy tariff adjustments required by the IMF for Q4 2026 could create a temporary inflation pulse, potentially pushing CPI back above 15% before resuming the decline.

The PTI-Military Confrontation

Pakistan's political landscape since 2022 has been defined by the confrontation between PTI (Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf) founder Imran Khan and the military establishment. Khan's ouster via a no-confidence vote in April 2022 and subsequent arrest in August 2023 created the most significant civil-military tensions in decades. PTI's electoral success in the February 2024 elections (PTI-aligned independents won the most seats) demonstrated continued public support despite the party's ban from official participation.

CPEC: Anchor and Burden

The China-Pakistan Economic Corridor ($62B committed) is simultaneously Pakistan's most significant infrastructure investment and one of its most significant debt burdens. Phase 1 energy projects (coal, hydro, solar) have added approximately 8,000 MW of generation capacity but created a circular debt problem: capacity payments to Chinese IPPs in USD are creating FX drain even as domestic power tariffs remain politically constrained.

CPEC's Gwadar Port development gives China strategic maritime access to the Arabian Sea — a geopolitical anchor that makes Pakistan's stability a Chinese strategic interest. This creates an implicit backstop: China's bilateral deposits and debt rollovers have been a critical component of Pakistan's reserve stabilization, distinct from IMF flows.

For US investors, CPEC creates the FLEX/AVGO ops exposure flag: Pakistan's manufacturing and supply chain integration creates minor positive CPEC channel sensitivity, offset by the dominant negative FX and IMF stress channels. CPEC disruption (political or security-driven) would be a negative signal for China's BRI portfolio but a mild positive for supply chain diversification.
