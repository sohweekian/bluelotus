ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This thesis develops Prospective Event Intelligence (PEI), a Bayesian
scenario-tree framework for transforming current market, macroeconomic,
geopolitical, liquidity, narrative, and portfolio-state information into
probability-weighted future event pathways. PEI is an extension of the
BlueLotus Adaptive Cognitive Market System (ACMS), which already classifies
market states, records forecasts, enforces governance, and preserves
institutional memory.

The central problem is that most investment systems observe the present better
than they prepare for the future. They identify regimes, detect catalysts, and
generate reports, but they frequently fail to answer the CIO's most important
operational question:

   What are the next plausible event pathways, how likely are they, how durable
   are they, what market transmission channels do they activate, which portfolio
   sleeves benefit or suffer, what action is allowed before confirmation, and
   what kill condition invalidates the path?

The thesis proposes that financial markets under uncertain catalysts should not
be forecast as single price trajectories. They should be forecast as
probability-weighted event trees. A security's future return is not merely a
function of valuation, momentum, or sentiment. It is a conditional response to
event survival, regime transition, liquidity transmission, institutional
positioning, and reflexive feedback.

This refined edition preserves the five original contributions of the first
edition — the Event-Transition Inefficiency Conjecture, Narrative Survival
Probability, Event-to-Sleeve Transmission Theory, the Preparedness Alpha
Hypothesis, and the Law-Bound Forecasting Doctrine — and extends them with four
new theoretical instruments developed for this edition:

   (6) The Reflexive Suppression Theorem, which formalizes how a structurally
       unrelated liquidity event (e.g., a large private-market capital raise)
       can mechanically depress the price of a thematically-linked public proxy
       without invalidating that proxy's fundamental thesis — observed directly
       in the BlueLotus ASTS / SpaceX case.

   (7) The Hawkes Event-Excitation Model of catalyst clustering, importing
       self-exciting point-process theory to model how one geopolitical or
       policy event raises the conditional intensity of follow-on events, with
       a branching-ratio diagnostic for regime endogeneity.

   (8) The Oscillation-Engine Mean-Reversion Estimator, a quantitative
       characterization of a single-name tactical cash-generation sleeve, with
       empirical calibration on six months of BlueLotus ASTS daily data.

   (9) The Calibration-Resolution-Sharpness (CRS) Decomposition Protocol for
       forecast accountability, importing the Murphy/DeGroot-Fienberg/
       Gneiting-Raftery proper-scoring-rule literature to give the BlueLotus
       Brier ledger a rigorous decomposition into reliability, resolution, and
       irreducible uncertainty.

Empirically, the thesis proposes a mixed-method research design combining event
studies, Bayesian updating, survival analysis, regime-switching models,
Hawkes-process estimation, Granger-style lead-lag testing, Brier and logarithmic
scoring with full decomposition, walk-forward backtesting, qualitative process
tracing, and superforecasting tournament methodology. The framework is
falsifiable: if PEI does not improve probabilistic calibration, scenario
classification, drawdown control, or decision quality versus baseline ACMS and
naive benchmark models, the thesis fails.

2.1 EFFICIENT MARKETS AND THE PROBLEM OF INFORMATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Efficient market theory (Fama) argues that prices reflect available information.
The Grossman-Stiglitz paradox sharpens the tension: if prices were perfectly
informative, informed research would not be rewarded, and so no one would
produce it — which means prices cannot be perfectly informative. Costly
information production is therefore incompatible with strong-form efficiency.
This thesis accepts neither naive efficiency nor naive irrationality. It treats
markets as partially informative, partially distorted, and regime-dependent.

PEI therefore asks not whether markets are efficient in general, but: which
information is priced now, which is partially priced, and which future pathway
is not yet priced? The Event-Transition Inefficiency Conjecture (Chapter 3)
locates the fund's edge precisely in the gap between first-order pricing
(fast) and second-order pathway pricing (slow).

2.2 EVENT STUDIES AND EVENT-TRANSITION STUDIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Event-study methodology (MacKinlay; Campbell-Lo-MacKinlay) provides the
foundation for measuring abnormal returns around identifiable events. Standard
event studies measure abnormal returns over a window around the event date. PEI
extends this logic: the first reaction window is insufficient. Some events
require survival windows of 24 hours, 48 hours, 5 trading days, 20 trading days,
and 60 trading days. A ceasefire headline, Fed press conference, BOJ policy
shift, IPO liquidity event, sanctions announcement, or credit shock may have a
first-order impact and then a second-order reversal. PEI therefore converts
event studies into event-TRANSITION studies, measuring not just the jump but the
survival of the jump.

2.3 REGIME SWITCHING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Regime-switching models (Hamilton) show that time series can behave differently
across hidden states. PEI adds a portfolio-governance layer: the same event has
different meaning under different regimes. A hawkish Fed message during strong
growth may support banks; the same message during fragile liquidity may damage
high beta. A geopolitical de-escalation headline during credit calm may support
risk assets; the same headline during dollar pressure and growth-factor weakness
may only generate a short-covering rally. PEI therefore requires
regime-conditioned event interpretation. This is operationalized in the fund's
Event-Thesis Map, which already encodes, for example, that iran_deescalation
relates to the gold structural-inflation thesis as a "tactical relief window"
that lowers the immediate safe-haven bid without killing the structural thesis.

2.4 ADAPTIVE MARKETS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Adaptive Markets Hypothesis (Lo) reconciles efficient and behavioral views
by treating markets as evolving ecological systems. Participants adapt to
repeated shocks, learn from prior regimes, overfit recent lessons, and
occasionally become maladapted when the environment changes. The practical
implication for PEI: a model that worked in the last event class may fail in the
next if the adaptive ecology has changed. This is why PEI insists on
walk-forward validation and per-event-class sensitivity testing rather than a
single static model. It is also why the fund's doctrine that "tactical score
modifies timing, not structural thesis validity" is adaptive-markets-consistent:
it separates the slow-moving ecological thesis from the fast-moving tactical
state.

2.5 NARRATIVE ECONOMICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Narratives transmit across investors and alter economic behavior (Shiller). PEI
treats narratives as market objects with survival probabilities. A narrative is
not automatically tradable; it must survive long enough to become capital flow,
positioning change, volatility repricing, policy reaction, earnings revision,
or balance-sheet effect. Hence:

   Narrative value = probability x durability x transmission strength
                     x positioning imbalance.

2.6 REFLEXIVITY AND FEEDBACK (NEW)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Soros's theory of reflexivity holds that there is a two-way feedback loop
between participants' biased perceptions and market outcomes: distorted views
lead to inappropriate actions that change the fundamentals the views were about.
Reflexive loops can be self-reinforcing (boom-bust) or self-correcting. The
boom-bust sequence proceeds in identifiable stages: an underlying trend emerges,
a prevailing bias reinforces it, the bias is not yet recognized as a bias, a
testing phase arrives, and finally the loop becomes self-defeating and unwinds.

Greenwood and Hanson and Jin formalize reflexivity in credit markets: during
credit booms, low default rates make investors believe future defaults will be
low, and in the near term this belief is self-fulfilling because cheap financing
prevents the defaults that would otherwise occur. The feedback loop is always
present but is stronger at some times than others.

PEI imports reflexivity in two ways. First, narrative survival probability is
explicitly a reflexive quantity: a narrative that attracts flow validates itself
until it does not. Second — and this is the genuinely new contribution of this
edition — reflexivity explains the BlueLotus ASTS/SpaceX observation. The
SpaceX private capital raise (a USD 20 billion-plus bond program plus an ongoing
IPO pipeline) mechanically drains liquidity from publicly-traded space proxies
by reallocating capital toward the private vehicle. This is a NEGATIVE reflexive
externality on the proxy: a structurally unrelated event suppresses the proxy's
price without touching its fundamentals. The Reflexive Suppression Theorem
(Chapter 3) formalizes this and gives the CIO a test to distinguish a
mechanically-suppressed-but-valid name from a genuinely-broken one.

2.7 INFORMATION CASCADES AND HERDING (NEW)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer, and Welch's theory of informational cascades shows
that rational agents observing predecessors' actions (but not their private
signals) may optimally discard their own private information and follow the
crowd. Once a cascade starts, public information stops accumulating and
followers herd regardless of their private signals. Banerjee's herd model and
Scharfstein-Stein's reputational-herding model (managers herd to avoid the
career risk of being wrong alone) provide complementary mechanisms. Wermers
provides evidence that mutual-fund herding influences prices.

The relevance to PEI is direct and operational. The BlueLotus institutional-flow
layer repeatedly shows a correct fundamental signal coexisting with contradicting
institutional flow — for example, a name whose thesis is intact while
super-large and large tranches distribute. Cascade theory explains why: flow can
be a herding artifact that temporarily overrides fundamentals. This justifies
the fund's doctrine of treating price-without-flow-confirmation as provisional,
and it gives a theoretical basis for the kill condition
"institutional_outflows_worsen_after_price_bounce."

2.8 SELF-EXCITING POINT PROCESSES (NEW)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Hawkes process (Hawkes, 1971) is a self-exciting point process whose
conditional intensity rises when an event occurs and decays between events:

   lambda(t) = mu + SUM_{t_i < t} alpha * exp(-beta * (t - t_i))

where mu is the baseline intensity, alpha the excitation amplitude, and beta the
decay rate. Hawkes processes have been applied to volatility clustering,
order-flow dynamics, extreme-return occurrences, and financial contagion
(Bacry-Mastromatteo-Muzy; Ait-Sahalia et al. on mutually-exciting jumps across
international indices; Errais-Giesecke-Goldberg on correlated default). A central
diagnostic is the BRANCHING RATIO n = alpha/beta (for the exponential kernel),
the average number of first-generation offspring per event. Filimonov and
Sornette use the branching ratio as a measure of market ENDOGENEITY: as n -> 1,
the market is near-critical and most activity is internally generated
(reflexive) rather than driven by exogenous news; empirical estimates in
high-frequency equity data have risen toward 0.7-0.9 over recent decades.

PEI imports the Hawkes framework to model CATALYST clustering: a geopolitical
escalation raises the conditional intensity of follow-on escalations; a single
hawkish central-bank surprise raises the intensity of repricing events across
rates, FX, and credit. The branching-ratio diagnostic gives the CIO a
quantitative handle on the question "is this move exogenous news or endogenous
reflexive cascade?" — which maps directly onto the fund's causal-verification
doctrine.

2.9 SUPERFORECASTING AND PROPER SCORING RULES (EXTENDED)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Superforecasting research (Tetlock and Mellers; the Good Judgment Project)
demonstrates the value of decomposing uncertain events into explicit
probabilistic questions, updating frequently, avoiding overconfidence, and
scoring forecasts. PEI imports this into portfolio governance: every scenario
branch must have a probability, a resolution date, observable resolution
criteria, a Brier score, a calibration record, and a post-mortem. A forecast
that cannot be scored is not a forecast; it is commentary.

This edition extends the scoring foundation using the proper-scoring-rule
literature. The Brier score is a STRICTLY PROPER scoring rule (Gneiting and
Raftery): its expectation is optimized if and only if the forecaster reports the
true probability, so it cannot be gamed. Crucially, the Brier score admits the
Murphy decomposition (also DeGroot-Fienberg; Dawid) into three interpretable
terms:

   Brier = Reliability - Resolution + Uncertainty

where Reliability (calibration) measures the average mismatch between forecast
probabilities and realized frequencies (lower is better), Resolution measures
how far conditional outcome frequencies depart from the base rate (higher is
better — it is the discrimination the forecaster adds), and Uncertainty is the
irreducible variance of the outcome (a property of the problem, not the
forecaster). The Gneiting-Raftery sharpness principle states the goal precisely:
maximize sharpness (resolution) SUBJECT TO calibration. The CRS Decomposition
Protocol (Chapter 6) operationalizes this for the BlueLotus Brier ledger so that
when forecasts mature, the fund can diagnose WHETHER a poor score reflects
miscalibration (fixable by recalibration) or low resolution (fixable only by
better signals).

2.10 BAYESIAN MODEL AVERAGING AND FORECAST COMBINATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Financial markets contain multiple competing explanations; a single model is
rarely sufficient. Bayesian model averaging (Steel; Raftery et al.) provides a
formal way to combine competing models and penalize overconfidence. PEI avoids
one-model dominance, allowing a macro-policy hypothesis, a liquidity hypothesis,
a credit hypothesis, a positioning hypothesis, a narrative hypothesis, a
technical-break hypothesis, and a portfolio-flow hypothesis. The final scenario
probability is a weighted posterior, not a single model output. This mirrors the
V3 nine-agent council design, which is itself an informal model-averaging
device: the Macro Strategist, Risk Challenger, Sentiment Narrative, and other
agents are competing models whose disagreement is logged and escalated rather
than silently averaged away.

2.11 BACKTEST OVERFITTING AND DATA SNOOPING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Financial research is vulnerable to false discoveries (Bailey-Lopez de Prado on
the deflated Sharpe ratio and backtest overfitting; White's Reality Check;
Harvey-Liu-Zhu on multiple-testing in cross-sectional asset pricing). PEI must
therefore include explicit safeguards: walk-forward validation, out-of-sample
testing, purged and embargoed cross-validation (Lopez de Prado), the White
Reality Check, false-discovery-rate control, the deflated Sharpe ratio,
sensitivity analysis, and pre-registered hypotheses. No PEI claim is accepted
because it worked once in a dramatic case study. This is the methodological
firewall that separates the thesis from narrative confirmation.

3.2 THE EVENT-TRANSITION INEFFICIENCY CONJECTURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Conjecture 1: Markets price first-order event headlines faster than
second-order event transitions.

Example:
   First-order:  Iran peace deal announced.
   Second-order: Does the deal survive inspections, shipping insurance,
                 sanctions relief, oil normalization, domestic political
                 backlash, and Fed inflation repricing?

The first-order headline may be priced within minutes. The second-order pathway
may take days or weeks. This creates the window in which PEI operates. The
conjecture is testable: if true, abnormal returns conditional on second-order
resolution should be predictable from PEI survival forecasts beyond what
first-order headline polarity predicts. If false, headline polarity alone should
subsume the survival signal in a horse-race regression. The test is specified in
Chapter 6.

3.3 NARRATIVE SURVIVAL PROBABILITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

An event is not binary. A peace deal, Fed pivot, BOJ move, IPO liquidity event,
or regulatory action has a SURVIVAL CURVE. Let event E_i be observed at time t0.
Define survival over horizon h:

   P_i(h) = P( E_i still market-valid at t0 + h | X_t0 )

where X_t0 includes source credibility, counterparty incentives, historical
failure rate, cross-market confirmation, volatility response, institutional
flow, policy constraints, and contradiction signals. Survival is estimated by
the discrete-time hazard model of Section 6.3.

3.4 EVENT VALUE EQUATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The investable value of an event is:

   V(E_i) = P(S_i) * D_i * T_i * I_i * G_i  -  R_i

where:
   P(S_i) = event survival probability
   D_i    = durability (narrative half-life; Section 12.2)
   T_i    = transmission strength (how forcefully it reaches prices/flows)
   I_i    = positioning imbalance (how crowded the opposite side is)
   G_i    = governance permission (1 if allowed, 0 if blocked)
   R_i    = residual risk / kill-condition penalty

This equation formalizes a key BlueLotus doctrine: a catalyst is not enough. It
must survive, transmit, confirm, and fit governance. Note the multiplicative
governance gate: if G_i = 0 the entire value collapses to -R_i regardless of how
attractive the other factors are. PEI is structurally incapable of recommending
a governance-blocked action, by construction of the value function itself.

3.5 SCENARIO-TREE MODEL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let B = {b_1, ..., b_n} be scenario branches. Each branch has probability p_b,
expected return r_b, expected shortfall ES_b, and governance permission g_b. The
portfolio action a should maximize a governance-penalized, risk-averse utility:

   U(a) = SUM_b p_b * R(a,b)
        - lambda * SUM_b p_b * ES(a,b)
        - C(a)
        - kappa * SUM_b p_b * K(a,b)

where R(a,b) is return under action a if branch b occurs, ES(a,b) is expected
shortfall, C(a) is concentration/turnover/liquidity cost, K(a,b) is the
kill-condition violation penalty, and lambda, kappa are risk-aversion and
governance-penalty coefficients. If g_b = 0 for the action under branch b, the
action is blocked regardless of expected return. PEI is not a profit-maximizing
machine; it is a law-bound portfolio-preparation system.

3.6 THE REFLEXIVE SUPPRESSION THEOREM (NEW)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Motivation. On the live BlueLotus book, ASTS (a publicly-traded space/satellite
name) fell 5.58 percent on a session in which SPY rose 0.78 percent, QQQ rose
2.51 percent, and the broad relief rally from the confirmed Iran-Hormuz MOU was
otherwise intact. The space/defense theme basket was selectively risk-off
(-1.61 percent) while AI/semis (+5.31 percent) and quantum (+4.34 percent)
rallied. Simultaneously, Bloomberg reported a SpaceX bond program of at least
USD 20 billion and an ongoing private fundraising campaign. The naive reading is
"space thesis broken." The correct reading is reflexive suppression.

Statement. Let a public proxy P be thematically linked to a private vehicle Q
(here, ASTS and SpaceX share the commercial-space theme). Let a large primary
capital event occur at Q that absorbs marginal thematic capital. Then, over the
absorption window, the proxy P can exhibit negative abnormal returns driven by
liquidity reallocation rather than by any change in P's fundamentals or
forward cash flows.

Formalization. Decompose the proxy's return into a thematic-fundamental
component and a liquidity-flow component:

   r_P = beta_theme * r_theme  +  gamma * dL_P  +  epsilon

where dL_P is the change in marginal thematic capital available to P. A primary
event at Q induces dL_P < 0 (capital is reallocated from public proxies to the
private vehicle). If |gamma * dL_P| > |beta_theme * r_theme|, then r_P < 0 even
when r_theme > 0. The proxy falls in a rising thematic tape. This is a flow
artifact, not a fundamental signal.

Diagnostic test (the discriminator the CIO needs). Reflexive suppression is
present, rather than genuine thesis breakdown, when ALL of the following hold:

   (i)   the thematic basket ex-proxy is flat-to-up while the proxy is down;
   (ii)  a documented primary capital event at the linked private vehicle
         coincides with the absorption window;
   (iii) the proxy's own fundamental catalysts (contracts, earnings, regulatory
         milestones) are unchanged;
   (iv)  the proxy's price reverts toward its mean once the absorption window
         closes (testable ex post);
   (v)   institutional outflow in the proxy is consistent with reallocation
         (rotational) rather than capitulation (one-directional acceleration
         with rising volatility and credit stress).

If instead the thematic basket is broadly down, fundamental catalysts have
deteriorated, and outflow is accelerating with credit stress, the suppression
diagnosis fails and a genuine-breakdown diagnosis is warranted, which would
escalate toward a kill condition.

Governance consequence. Under reflexive suppression, the correct action class is
HOLD / OBSERVE or, for a name the CIO knows behaviorally, PULLBACK-ONLY ADD into
the suppression — NOT de-risk. The theorem thereby protects the CIO from being
shaken out of a valid position by a mechanical liquidity drain. It also bounds
the protection: criterion (iv) is falsifiable ex post, so a suppression call
that does not mean-revert is automatically logged as a forecasting error and
fed to the Brier ledger.

3.7 THE HAWKES EVENT-EXCITATION MODEL (NEW)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PEI models the catalyst stream as a marked Hawkes process. Let events arrive
with conditional intensity:

   lambda(t) = mu + SUM_{t_i < t} alpha(m_i) * exp(-beta * (t - t_i))

where m_i is the MARK of event i (its magnitude/severity — e.g., the size of a
geopolitical shock or the surprise in a policy statement), and alpha(m_i) scales
excitation by the mark. The branching ratio for the exponential kernel is
n = E[alpha(m)] / beta.

Interpretation for the CIO:
   - n near 0:   events are mostly exogenous, independent news arrivals; the
                 tape is news-driven and a single resolved catalyst is unlikely
                 to spawn a cascade.
   - n near 1:   the regime is near-critical and reflexive; most "new" events
                 are endogenously triggered by prior events. A single shock can
                 ignite a self-sustaining cascade (the mechanism behind flash
                 crashes and contagion).

The branching ratio operationalizes the difference between "one peace headline"
and "a peace headline that triggers an oil repricing that triggers an inflation
repricing that triggers a Fed repricing." It is the quantitative form of the
fund's Event Gravity Principle (Section 12.3). Estimation uses maximum-likelihood
on the catalyst-timestamp series with the standard Ozaki recursion for the
exponential kernel, with the Filimonov-Sornette endogeneity caveat that
mis-specified kernels bias n upward — so power-law and sum-of-exponential kernels
are fitted as robustness checks.

3.8 THE OSCILLATION-ENGINE MEAN-REVERSION ESTIMATOR (NEW)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Several BlueLotus sleeves are not buy-and-hold positions but tactical
cash-generation engines: trim into strength, reload into weakness, harvest the
oscillation. The PL/ASTS sleeve is the canonical example. PEI formalizes the
behavior of such a sleeve so that the CIO's discretionary edge can be measured
rather than asserted.

Let a single-name engine have a slow-moving central tendency mu_t (its
behavioral "usual price") and fast mean-reverting deviations. A discrete
Ornstein-Uhlenbeck representation:

   P_{t+1} - P_t = theta * (mu_t - P_t) + sigma * z_t

where theta is the speed of mean reversion, sigma the volatility, and z_t a
shock. The engine's expected one-step return from price P_t is
theta * (mu_t - P_t): positive when price is below the central tendency,
negative when above. The optimal oscillation policy (trim above mu_t, reload
below) is the discrete analogue of harvesting the OU drift, bounded by the
sleeve's capital cap and the Thorp free-rider rule (trim to realized profit at
consensus, hold a stub for convexity).

Empirical calibration on six months of BlueLotus ASTS daily data (Dec 2025 -
Jun 2026, 132 trading days) is reported in Chapter 5 and yields a central
tendency near USD 89-90 (mean close 89.64, median 87.55), an average daily
absolute move of 6.2 percent, an average daily high-low range of 10.1 percent,
and a support zone (10th-25th percentile of closes) of roughly USD 73-81. These
parameters make the name a textbook oscillation engine: high sigma, identifiable
mu_t, repeated tests of the support band. The estimator gives the CIO's "buy the
low, it usually mean-reverts to ninety" intuition a defensible quantitative
form, and — critically — a falsifiable one: if reload-zone entries stop
mean-reverting to mu_t, the engine is logged as regime-broken.

CHAPTER 6 — QUANTITATIVE METHODOLOGY (EXTENDED)
================================================================================

6.1 BAYESIAN UPDATING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For each event branch b, the posterior probability is:

   P(b | X_t) = [ P(X_t | b) * P(b) ] / P(X_t)

where X_t is real-time evidence grouped as macro, credit, volatility, flow,
price, narrative, portfolio, and governance evidence. Updates are triggered by
the rules of Section 8.3 (new macro data, policy statements, volatility-proxy
threshold crossings, credit moves, oil moves, USD/JPY moves, flow changes, price
confirmation/contradiction, or kill-condition activation).

6.2 BAYESIAN MODEL AVERAGING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Each model M_k produces a branch probability P(b | X_t, M_k). The final PEI
probability is:

   P(b | X_t) = SUM_k P(b | X_t, M_k) * P(M_k | D)

Models include the regime model, event-survival model, flow model, credit model,
volatility model, narrative model, options-flow model, and portfolio-state
model. Model weights P(M_k | D) are themselves updated by each model's realized
log-score, so persistently miscalibrated models lose weight over time. This is
the formal counterpart to the V3 council's disagreement-logging: models that are
repeatedly wrong are down-weighted, not silently retained.

6.3 SURVIVAL ANALYSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Event durability is modeled with a discrete-time hazard. For event i, define
failure as catalyst invalidation (peace deal walked back; VXX reverses;
oil-risk premium returns; credit stress appears; high beta fades; Fed repricing
overwhelms relief; governance kill condition triggered). The discrete-time
hazard:

   P( F_{i,t} = 1 | F_{i,t-1} = 0, X_t ) = logit^{-1}( a + b' * X_t )

The survival curve P_i(h) of Section 3.3 is the product of period-by-period
non-failure probabilities. Continuous-time alternatives (Cox proportional
hazards) are fitted as robustness checks, with the caveat that the
proportional-hazards assumption is tested (Schoenfeld residuals) rather than
assumed.

6.4 EVENT-STUDY TESTING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For each event class: define event date t0; estimate normal returns over a
pre-event window; compute abnormal returns; compute cumulative abnormal returns
over [-1,+1], [0,+1], [0,+5], [0,+20]; and compare branches (confirmed event,
failed event, policy-override event, credit-stress-override event). Cross-
sectional and time-series standard errors are corrected for event-window
overlap and clustering.

6.5 LEAD-LAG TESTING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For signal class s, test whether s_t predicts branch realization or abnormal
return at horizon h:

   Y_{t+h} = a + b_s * S_t + b_r * R_t + b_c * C_t + e_t

where Y is the branch outcome or abnormal return, S_t the signal, R_t regime
controls, C_t cross-market controls. Newey-West standard errors handle
autocorrelation and heteroskedasticity. Granger-causality is interpreted
strictly as predictive precedence, not structural causation; process tracing
(Chapter 7) supplies the causal narrative that statistics alone cannot.

6.6 HAWKES BRANCHING-RATIO ESTIMATION (NEW)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The catalyst-timestamp series is fitted with a marked exponential-kernel Hawkes
process by maximum likelihood (Ozaki recursion). The branching ratio
n = E[alpha(m)]/beta is reported with bootstrap confidence intervals.
Robustness: power-law and sum-of-two-exponential kernels are fitted to guard
against the Filimonov-Sornette upward bias in n under kernel mis-specification.
The estimated n at event onset becomes a regressor in H8 (endogeneity predicts
realized volatility and cascade count).

6.7 FORECAST EVALUATION WITH CRS DECOMPOSITION (NEW / EXTENDED)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Brier score:

   BS = (1/N) * SUM_i ( f_i - o_i )^2

with f_i the forecast probability and o_i the realized outcome (1/0). The Brier
score is strictly proper (Gneiting-Raftery): it is minimized in expectation only
by truthful probabilities, so it cannot be gamed. The Murphy decomposition
(DeGroot-Fienberg) partitions the mean Brier score into:

   BS = Reliability - Resolution + Uncertainty

   Reliability (calibration loss): average squared gap between forecast
      probabilities and the conditional outcome frequencies in each probability
      bin. LOWER is better. A reliability term near zero means "when the system
      says 70 percent, it happens about 70 percent of the time."
   Resolution: average squared gap between bin outcome frequencies and the
      overall base rate. HIGHER is better. It is the discrimination the system
      adds beyond always predicting the base rate.
   Uncertainty: variance of the outcome, o_bar*(1-o_bar). A property of the
      problem, not the forecaster; it is the irreducible floor.

The Gneiting-Raftery sharpness principle gives the operational target: MAXIMIZE
RESOLUTION SUBJECT TO CALIBRATION. The protocol therefore reports all three
components for every model and every event class, plus the logarithmic score
(which penalizes confident errors more harshly and is the natural score for
early-warning, long-horizon forecasts), reliability diagrams, expected
calibration error, ROC-AUC for binary questions, and precision/recall for rare
events. This converts the fund's single Brier scalar into a diagnostic: a poor
score caused by high reliability loss is fixed by recalibration; a poor score
caused by low resolution is fixed only by better signals — two different
remedies that the undecomposed scalar cannot distinguish.

6.8 STATISTICAL TESTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Required: Brier-difference test; Diebold-Mariano forecast-accuracy test;
event-study t-tests with robust errors; nonparametric bootstrap; multiple-
hypothesis correction (Benjamini-Hochberg FDR and, for strategy selection,
the White Reality Check / Hansen SPA); deflated Sharpe ratio; per-event-class
sensitivity; out-of-sample walk-forward performance; strategy-level drawdown
and expected-shortfall tests; and forecast-calibration stability across
sub-periods.

6.9 ANTI-OVERFITTING PROTOCOL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Pre-registered hypotheses; frozen feature set per test window; no look-ahead;
no survivorship bias; point-in-time data; explicit transaction-cost and slippage
assumptions; walk-forward validation; purged and embargoed cross-validation
(Lopez de Prado) around overlapping event windows; White Reality Check for
strategy selection. No result is accepted unless reproducible. The fund's
existing point-in-time-readiness telemetry is the substrate for this protocol;
the thesis's contribution is to bind it to pre-registration so that the
program cannot be retroactively tuned to fit the events it studied.

CHAPTER 7 — QUALITATIVE METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

7.1 WHY QUALITATIVE RESEARCH IS NECESSARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Markets are not purely numerical. Events contain ambiguity that cannot be
measured immediately: policy credibility, diplomatic incentives, leadership
psychology, institutional behavior, media-narrative drift, investor attention,
uncertainty avoidance, and reflexive crowd behavior. PEI therefore requires
qualitative process tracing. The reflexivity and cascade literatures
(Chapter 2) are precisely theories of phenomena that resist purely quantitative
identification in real time; process tracing is how the fund reasons about them
before the statistics resolve.

7.2 PROCESS-TRACING PROTOCOL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For each major event: record the initial event; identify competing hypotheses;
record evidence for and against each; build a timeline; track probability
updates; record the CIO decision; record portfolio action or non-action;
resolve the outcome; conduct a post-mortem. This is the Bayesian-process-tracing
discipline (Bennett; Beach-Pedersen) applied to portfolio decisions, with the
explicit requirement that evidence both FOR and AGAINST each hypothesis be
logged — the antidote to confirmation bias.

7.3 CAUSAL NARRATIVE AUDIT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Each final report must ask: Did we confuse headline with durable catalyst? Did
we confuse relief rally with regime change? Did we confuse scout with second
tranche? Did we overfit one historical analogy? Did we treat price as
confirmation without flow? Did we ignore credit? FX? Volatility? Did we
preserve cash when uncertainty remained? Did we follow law-bound governance?
Did we mistake reflexive suppression for thesis breakdown (NEW)? Did we mistake
an endogenous cascade for exogenous news (NEW)?

CHAPTER 10 — BACKTESTING DESIGN
================================================================================

10.1 OBJECTIVE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The backtest does not test whether PEI predicts every market move. It tests
whether PEI improves PORTFOLIO PREPARATION under uncertainty. Primary objective:
reduce severe decision errors during uncertain catalysts. Secondary: improve
Brier score, log score, drawdown, expected shortfall, action discipline, and
post-event regret.

10.2 STRATEGIES COMPARED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

   Strategy 1 — Naive baseline (equal branch probabilities).
   Strategy 2 — Regime-only ACMS (posture from current regime only).
   Strategy 3 — Event-headline model (posture from catalyst polarity only).
   Strategy 4 — PEI scenario tree (survival, transmission, confirmation, kill
                conditions, sleeve mapping, reflexive-suppression and
                branching-ratio overlays).
   Strategy 5 — PEI with governance blocks (Strategy 4 constrained by sleeve
                rules, exposure caps, and CIO_ONLY_MANUAL doctrine).

10.3 PORTFOLIO SIMULATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Research-only; no automated execution implied. For each event: create the
forecast at t0; assign branch probabilities; generate the allowed posture;
simulate the action under predefined rules; apply transaction costs and
slippage; record outcomes at 1D, 5D, 20D, 60D; score the forecast (with CRS
decomposition at the aggregate); score the portfolio result; record the
post-mortem.

10.4 PERFORMANCE METRICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Forecast: Brier (decomposed into reliability/resolution/uncertainty), log score,
calibration, resolution, sharpness. Portfolio: total return, event-window
return, max drawdown, expected shortfall, volatility, Sharpe, Sortino, Calmar,
turnover, exposure utilization, hit rate, regret. Governance: bad trades
blocked, good trades missed, rule violations, overexposure incidents,
contradiction count, unauthorized-execution-language count.

10.5 ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PEI is accepted only if it achieves at least THREE of the following
out-of-sample: lower Brier than baseline; lower log loss; lower max drawdown;
lower expected shortfall; better calibration (lower reliability term); lower
false-positive add rate; lower post-event regret; fewer governance
contradictions; better scenario-classification accuracy; higher utility after
costs.

10.6 FALSIFICATION CRITERIA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The thesis FAILS if: scenario-tree forecasts do not outperform naive or
regime-only forecasts; event-survival probabilities are not calibrated; kill
conditions block more good trades than bad ones; playbooks increase drawdowns;
results vanish after transaction costs; results fail out-of-sample; results
depend on cherry-picked events; backtest significance disappears under the White
Reality Check; the system produces contradictory governance advice; the human
CIO cannot understand or audit the outputs; reflexive-suppression flags do not
predict mean reversion better than chance (NEW); or the branching ratio does not
predict realized volatility (NEW).

12.1 PREPAREDNESS ALPHA HYPOTHESIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Alpha does not always come from predicting the most likely future; sometimes it
comes from preparing rationally for several futures before others have mapped
them.

   A_p = V(P_prepared) - V(P_reactive)

where V(P_prepared) is the value of a pre-mapped scenario posture and
V(P_reactive) the value of reacting after confirmation. Preparedness alpha is
the value of optionality plus the avoided cost of forced, late, expensive
reaction.

12.2 NARRATIVE HALF-LIFE THEORY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Every market narrative has a half-life. Short half-life narratives produce
gap-fade behavior; long half-life narratives produce multi-session repricing.
Half-life is estimated from source credibility, repetition intensity,
institutional adoption, cross-asset confirmation, contradiction frequency,
policy durability, and capital-flow confirmation. The half-life maps directly
to the hazard rate of Section 6.3: a long half-life is a low failure hazard.

12.3 EVENT GRAVITY PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Some events exert gravitational force across multiple sleeves. A hawkish Fed
affects high beta, banks, gold, the dollar, credit, volatility, liquidity, and
cash value simultaneously. The greater the number of affected sleeves, the
higher the event gravity. Event gravity is the multi-sleeve analogue of the
Hawkes branching ratio: a high-gravity event is one whose excitation propagates
across many transmission channels, raising the conditional intensity of
follow-on repricings in each.

12.4 LATENT RISK INCUBATION PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Some signals are early but not tradable: consumer-savings deterioration,
private-market liquidity drain, yen-carry pressure, sovereign-debt interest
burden, fiscal dominance, credit-stress divergence. These should not force
immediate trading; they are incubated with future review dates. The SpaceX
private-market liquidity drain is a live incubated signal: it is real, it
suppresses proxies reflexively, and it warrants a scheduled review rather than
an immediate trade.

12.5 LAW-BOUND AI COGNITION PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

AI forecasting must be structurally subordinate to institutional law. Without
law, AI becomes narrative drift. With law, AI becomes controlled synthesis. This
principle is not decorative: the multiplicative governance gate in the Event
Value Equation (Section 3.4) and the g_b = 0 blocking term in the scenario-tree
utility (Section 3.5) make law-violation mathematically dominated, not merely
discouraged.

12.6 THE REFLEXIVE SUPPRESSION THEOREM (CROSS-REFERENCE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Formalized in Section 3.6. Its place among the original contributions: it is the
first BlueLotus construct to give the CIO a falsifiable test for the difference
between a name that is DOWN because it is BROKEN and a name that is DOWN because
a structurally-unrelated private vehicle is absorbing its thematic capital. The
distinction is worth real money: in the ASTS case it is the difference between
de-risking a valid oscillation engine at the bottom of its support band and
holding (or adding into) a mechanically-suppressed name that mean-reverts to ~90.

12.7 THE ENDOGENEITY-AWARENESS PRINCIPLE (NEW)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Derived from the Hawkes branching ratio. A move should be interpreted
differently depending on whether it is exogenously driven (news) or endogenously
driven (reflexive cascade). Near-critical regimes (branching ratio near 1) are
fragile: small shocks can ignite large cascades, and apparent "news" may be
mostly internally generated noise. In such regimes the correct posture tilts
toward hedge-retention and cash-preservation, because the tail of self-excited
cascades is fat. This principle gives the volatility-hedge sleeve a rigorous
trigger beyond simple VIX level.
