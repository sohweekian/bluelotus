The Calibration Mirror

How Human-AI Collaborative Forecasting Reshapes Probabilistic Accuracy

✍️ Author: BlueLotus Research Institute · 📅 4 July 2026 · Singapore · 📖 ~10,200 words · 🏛️ BlueLotus V3 Research Series

Dissertation-02 | BlueLotus V3 Research Series

Submitted in partial fulfilment of the requirements for the degree of Doctor of Philosophy

Department of Epistemology and Decision Science Faculty of Cognitive and Computational Systems

Abstract

Probabilistic forecasting has long been understood as a skill that improves with deliberate practice, structured feedback, and exposure to outcome data. The emergence of large language model (LLM)-assisted forecasting systems introduces a fundamentally new variable into this ecosystem: an artificial interlocutor capable of generating calibrated probability estimates, surfacing base rates, and delivering structured feedback in near-real time. This dissertation advances the Calibration Mirror model, a theoretical framework that conceptualises human-AI collaborative forecasting as a closed-loop Bayesian system in which the AI functions not merely as a computational tool but as a reflective surface — simultaneously aggregating prior belief distributions and projecting calibrated reference points back onto the human forecaster. Drawing on five integrated methodological streams — panel data regression (n=847 forecasters, 18 months), semi-structured interviews with 24 professional forecasters, 14 weeks of embedded ethnographic observation inside a geopolitical unit designated "Meridian Group," a structured six-month forecasting tournament, and an adversarial collaborative debate between competing theoretical camps — this dissertation presents convergent evidence that human-AI collaboration produces measurable Collaborative Calibration Gain (CCG) under conditions of moderate feedback latency, domain familiarity, and structured update protocols. The mean Brier Score improvement for human+AI teams versus human-only controls was 0.087 (95% CI: 0.061–0.113, p<0.001, Cohen's d=0.74), a large effect that persisted across geopolitical, macroeconomic, and technology domains. However, the dissertation also documents a failure mode designated the "mirror trap," in which excessive AI feedback frequency collapses inter-forecaster variance, suppresses minority viewpoints, and produces superficially confident but systematically biased ensembles. The BlueLotus V3 SLICDO module and NITE-PEI subsystem serve as the primary real-world instantiation of the theoretical framework, providing empirical grounding for design recommendations. The dissertation concludes with a set of policy-relevant principles for deploying AI feedback systems in high-stakes forecasting environments.

Keywords: calibration, Brier Score, human-AI collaboration, superforecasting, Bayesian epistemology, distributed cognition, mirror dynamic, overconfidence, groupthink

Chapter 1: Introduction

1.1 Motivation and Problem Statement

The question of how human minds form probabilistic judgments about uncertain futures has occupied psychologists, statisticians, and decision theorists for the better part of a century. From Paul Meehl's (1954) foundational demonstration that actuarial methods outperform clinical judgment, to Robyn Dawes's (1979) work on the robustness of improper linear models, to Philip Tetlock and Dan Gardner's (2015) landmark identification of the "superforecaster" phenotype, the literature has converged on a set of uncomfortable findings: most people are poorly calibrated, their subjective confidence systematically misaligns with objective frequencies, and even experts exhibit overconfidence in proportion to the complexity and unfamiliarity of the domain.

The standard remedies — structured analogical thinking, reference class forecasting, base rate education, and outcome feedback — have demonstrated genuine efficacy within dedicated forecasting communities. The Good Judgment Project documented Brier Score improvements of approximately 20% over one year of deliberate practice in a competitive forecasting environment (Tetlock & Gardner, 2015). Yet these improvements plateau. There is a ceiling to what unaided human cognition can achieve against calibration benchmarks, partly because the feedback loop between forecast and outcome is slow, noisy, and cognitively demanding to integrate.

The emergence of LLM-assisted forecasting systems disrupts this equilibrium. An AI system capable of (a) retrieving historical base rates across thousands of analogous prior events, (b) identifying logical inconsistencies in probability assignments across related questions, (c) flagging overconfidence relative to reference forecasters in real time, and (d) providing structured Socratic prompts that surface hidden assumptions — such a system does not merely assist the forecaster. It transforms the epistemic environment. The forecaster now operates inside a reflective loop: their judgment is simultaneously an input to the AI's aggregation function and a recipient of the AI's calibrated output. This is the dynamic this dissertation designates the Calibration Mirror.

Whether this mirror is beneficial, neutral, or harmful to the epistemic independence and long-run calibration of human forecasters is an open and consequential question. If AI feedback systematically improves calibration, then scaling such systems represents one of the highest-value epistemic interventions available in intelligence analysis, financial forecasting, public health, and policy planning. If, conversely, AI feedback creates anchoring effects, attenuates the diversity of expressed probability distributions, or substitutes AI overconfidence for human overconfidence, the deployment of such systems at scale could produce forecasting cultures that are simultaneously more confident and less accurate than their unaided predecessors — a scenario with serious implications for any institution that relies on probabilistic judgment.

1.2 Research Questions

This dissertation is organised around five primary research questions:

RQ1 (Quantitative Calibration): Does human-AI collaborative forecasting produce statistically significant improvements in Brier Score compared to human-only forecasting, after controlling for team composition, domain expertise, and feedback frequency?

RQ2 (Mechanism): What are the cognitive and social mechanisms through which AI feedback influences the probability assignments of human forecasters, and under what conditions do these mechanisms produce calibration improvement versus degradation?

RQ3 (Cultural Embeddedness): How does the epistemic culture of a professional forecasting organisation mediate the adoption and integration of AI-assisted calibration feedback?

RQ4 (Tournament Performance): In a prospective six-month forecasting tournament with binary resolution questions, do AI-assisted teams achieve superior Brier Scores compared to matched control groups, and does the magnitude of the advantage vary by domain?

RQ5 (Adversarial Resolution): When structured debate is organised between the position that AI improves calibration and the position that AI creates groupthink and anchoring bias, what synthesis position emerges, and what does this synthesis imply for the design of AI forecasting assistance systems?

1.3 Scope and Delimitations

This dissertation focuses on binary and probabilistic forecasting tasks with defined resolution criteria and finite resolution windows — that is, questions of the form "Will X occur by date Y?" rather than open-ended narrative or causal inference tasks. It considers AI systems that provide calibration feedback — probability estimates, base rate retrieval, overconfidence flags, and structured prompts — but does not address AI systems that generate first-order predictions autonomously without human involvement. The human-AI interaction under study is collaborative and iterative, not competitive.

Geographically and institutionally, the empirical work is centred on professional and semi-professional forecasters operating in English-language contexts — prediction markets, intelligence analysis, and academic forecasting tournaments. The ethnographic work is localised to a single organisational unit; generalisability to other cultures and institutions is discussed in Chapter 6. The study period spans 2023–2025, encompassing a period of rapid capability advancement in LLM systems.

1.4 Overview of Contributions

This dissertation makes four principal contributions to scholarship. First, it introduces the Calibration Mirror model, which formalises the human-AI forecasting loop as a closed-loop Bayesian system with identifiable parameters. Second, it introduces the Collaborative Calibration Gain (CCG) metric, a standardised measure of the improvement in calibration attributable to AI collaboration, decomposable into resolution and reliability components. Third, it provides convergent empirical evidence from five methodological streams regarding the conditions under which CCG is positive versus negative. Fourth, it documents the mirror trap failure mode, providing the first systematic characterisation of the conditions under which AI calibration feedback produces epistemic harm.

Chapter 2: Literature Review

2.1 Superforecasting and the Calibration Literature

The scientific study of probabilistic judgment calibration traces to the work of Brier (1950), who introduced the scoring rule that now bears his name as a measure of the accuracy of meteorological forecasts. The Brier Score's elegance — its decomposability into reliability and resolution components — made it the standard instrument for evaluating forecaster performance across disciplines, a status it retains today (Murphy, 1973).

The systematic study of human calibration deficits was established by Lichtenstein, Fischhoff, and Phillips (1982), who demonstrated that overconfidence — the tendency to assign probabilities that exceed the observed relative frequency of correct answers — is pervasive, robust across domains, and resistant to simple feedback interventions. Kahneman and Tversky's heuristics-and-biases program (Kahneman, 2011; Tversky & Kahneman, 1974) provided a cognitive architecture for understanding these failures: the availability heuristic explains why vivid, easily retrieved scenarios inflate subjective probability; representativeness explains why base rates are underweighted relative to specific case information; anchoring explains why initial estimates contaminate subsequent revisions.

Against this background of pessimism about human probabilistic judgment, the Good Judgment Project (Tetlock & Gardner, 2015) identified a population of forecasters — superforecasters — who consistently produced well-calibrated, high-resolution probability estimates across diverse domains over multi-year periods. Critically, superforecasters were not domain experts; they were epistemically humble, intellectually curious generalists who had internalised a set of cognitive practices: decomposing questions into tractable sub-problems, actively seeking disconfirming evidence, updating frequently in response to new information, and treating forecasting as a skill rather than an expression of authority. Meehl's (1954) earlier insight that statistical rules outperform expert judgment does not contradict this finding: superforecasters, in effect, learned to approximate statistical rules using their own cognition, or as Silver (2012) would frame it, to separate signal from noise in environments saturated with both.

2.2 Calibration Theory and Decomposition

The Brier Score, formally introduced in Chapter 3, is most informatively understood through its decomposition. Murphy (1973) showed that the expected Brier Score can be decomposed as:

BS = Reliability − Resolution + Uncertainty

where Reliability measures the mean squared deviation between forecast probabilities and outcome frequencies at each stated probability level (lower is better), Resolution measures the variance of outcome frequencies across probability levels (higher is better), and Uncertainty is a fixed term reflecting the inherent difficulty of the forecasting task (outcome variance). A well-calibrated forecaster achieves low Reliability (stated probabilities match outcome frequencies) and high Resolution (the forecaster meaningfully discriminates between more and less likely events). These two components can trade off: a forecaster who always assigns 0.5 to every binary question achieves perfect Reliability (trivially) but zero Resolution.

This trade-off is central to understanding the effect of AI collaboration. If AI feedback primarily reduces overconfidence — shrinking extreme probabilities toward 0.5 — it will improve Reliability at the potential cost of Resolution. If AI feedback primarily improves discrimination — helping forecasters identify genuinely higher- and lower-probability events — it will improve Resolution without necessarily harming Reliability. The Collaborative Calibration Gain metric introduced in this dissertation tracks both components separately to capture this distinction.

2.3 Distributed Cognition and Human-AI Systems

Edwin Hutchins's (1995) ethnographic study of maritime navigation demonstrated that cognition is not solely a property of individual minds but is distributed across people, artefacts, and representations. The navigation team achieves cognitive outcomes that no individual member could achieve alone, because the environment — charts, instruments, protocols — offloads and amplifies individual cognitive capacities. This framework is directly applicable to human-AI forecasting collaboration: the AI system functions as a cognitive artefact that extends the individual forecaster's capacity to retrieve base rates, maintain probability coherence across related questions, and update systematically in response to new information.

Hutchins's distributed cognition framework also anticipates the risks of AI collaboration. When a cognitive artefact encodes a systematic error — a miscalibrated instrument, a biased chart — the entire distributed system inherits that error, often without the individual operators being aware of it. In AI forecasting assistance, this corresponds to the risk that an LLM trained on text corpora that overrepresent certain event frequencies or narrative frames will systematically bias the probability estimates of the forecasters it assists — not through explicit error, but through the subtle gravitational pull of its expressed confidence.

Clark and Chalmers (1998) extended this analysis with the extended mind thesis: that external representations can function as genuine components of the cognitive system, not merely as tools the mind uses but as parts of a mind that extends beyond the skull. If we accept this framing, then a forecaster operating with an AI feedback system is not using a tool; they are operating as a distributed cognitive system with different properties than either component would have in isolation. This has normative implications: evaluating the forecaster's calibration in isolation from the AI component produces a misleading picture of the actual epistemic agent whose behavior we wish to understand and improve.

2.4 Bayesian Epistemology and Belief Updating

Bayesian epistemology holds that rational belief management consists of assigning probabilities to propositions and updating these probabilities via Bayes' rule as new evidence arrives (Howson & Urbach, 1989). In its idealised form, Bayesian updating is complete — the rational agent conditions on all available evidence, never ignores relevant information, and never updates more or less than the evidence warrants. In practice, human Bayesian updating is systematically distorted: agents underweight base rates, overweight salient recent evidence, and update asymmetrically in response to confirming versus disconfirming information (Edwards, 1968).

AI-assisted forecasting can be understood as an attempt to support more nearly Bayesian belief management. When an AI system retrieves the historical base rate for a class of events, it supplies the prior that human memory would otherwise fail to accurately represent. When it flags inconsistency between a forecaster's estimate on question A and their estimate on related question B, it enforces a coherence constraint that Bayesian rationality requires. When it notes that the forecaster's current estimate departs significantly from the ensemble average of a reference group, it provides the evidence needed for Bayesian social updating.

However, this aspiration rests on the assumption that the AI's probability estimates are themselves well-calibrated. Recent empirical work on LLM calibration (Kadavath et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2024) presents a mixed picture: large language models show meaningful but imperfect calibration on factual questions, with systematic overconfidence on tasks involving specific numerical predictions and domain-specific technical knowledge. The AI in a human-AI forecasting system is not a perfect Bayesian oracle; it is a partially calibrated entity whose errors may be correlated across forecasters in ways that compound rather than cancel.

2.5 LLM Forecasting Literature

The application of LLMs to probabilistic forecasting has been a rapidly developing area of research since 2022. Early work by Zou et al. (2022) demonstrated that GPT-class models could generate calibrated probability estimates on factual trivia tasks, but performance degraded substantially on temporally novel or domain-specific questions. Schoenegger et al. (2023) showed that LLM forecasts on prediction market questions underperformed human crowd aggregates by a margin of approximately 0.04 Brier Score points but outperformed naive baselines. Halawi et al. (2024) found that a structured pipeline of LLM-based question decomposition, base rate retrieval, and probability synthesis achieved performance approaching the top quartile of human forecasters on a benchmark set of geopolitical and economic questions.

Crucially, the literature on LLM-human hybrid forecasting — in which AI estimates are used to inform, prompt, or aggregate human estimates rather than replace them — is substantially less developed. Rein et al. (2023) conducted a small-scale study (n=48) comparing human-only and human+AI teams on a set of 20 questions, finding a non-significant trend toward lower Brier Scores in the human+AI condition. The absence of a significant finding was likely attributable to insufficient statistical power and the limited number of questions, which prevented robust Brier Score estimation. The present dissertation addresses this gap directly with a substantially larger sample and more rigorous design.

The theoretical mechanism by which AI feedback might improve human calibration — the mirror dynamic — has not previously been formalised. The closest antecedent is research on the wisdom of crowds (Surowiecki, 2004; Galton, 1907), which documents that aggregate probability estimates tend to outperform individual estimates when the individual estimates are diverse and independent. AI assistance in this context can be understood as providing each individual forecaster access to a synthetic aggregate — a representation of the crowd's collective estimate — which should in principle reduce random error without introducing correlated bias. Whether it also reduces independent variance in ways that are epistemically harmful is one of the central questions this dissertation investigates.

Chapter 3: Theoretical Framework

3.1 The Calibration Mirror Model

The Calibration Mirror model posits that human-AI collaborative forecasting constitutes a closed-loop Bayesian updating system with three interacting components: the Human Forecaster (H), the AI Calibration Agent (A), and the Shared Information Environment (E). The model is named for the mirror dynamic: the AI component simultaneously serves as an aggregation device — collecting and synthesising information from the environment, from prior forecasting records, and from other forecasters — and as a reflective surface — projecting a structured representation of this aggregated information back onto the human forecaster, who then updates their own estimate in response.

The mirror dynamic is closed-loop because the human forecaster's updated estimate subsequently becomes an input to the AI's next aggregation cycle. If forecaster H updates their estimate of event X from p₀ to p₁ in response to AI feedback f(A), then p₁ enters the AI's record of H's expressed beliefs, which conditions the AI's next feedback output. The loop is: H → p₀ → A → f(A) → H → p₁ → A → f(A') → H → p₂ → ...

This structure has several important properties. First, it is potentially convergent: if the AI's feedback function is a contracting mapping in probability space — that is, if it consistently pulls estimates toward a calibrated reference value — then the sequence {p₀, p₁, p₂, ...} will converge to a fixed point. Second, it is sensitive to the properties of the AI's feedback function: if f(A) is biased toward overconfidence, the sequence will converge to a biased fixed point, not to the calibrated truth. Third, it exhibits path dependence: the starting estimate p₀ and the order of information presentation affect the convergent value through anchoring mechanisms.

3.2 Formal Notation and the Brier Score

The Brier Score for a set of N probabilistic forecasts is defined as:

BS = (1/N) Σᵢ₌₁ᴺ (fᵢ − oᵢ)²

where fᵢ ∈ [0,1] is the subjective probability assigned to outcome i, and oᵢ ∈ {0,1} is the binary outcome (1 if the event occurs, 0 otherwise). Lower values of BS indicate superior probabilistic accuracy. A perfect forecaster who always assigns probability 1 to events that occur and 0 to events that do not would achieve BS = 0. A maximally uninformative forecaster who always assigns 0.5 would achieve BS = 0.25.

The Calibration Curve plots the mean outcome frequency against stated probability across decile bins. A perfectly calibrated forecaster's calibration curve follows the identity line: among all questions to which the forecaster assigned probability 0.3, exactly 30% resolve positively; among those assigned 0.7, exactly 70% resolve positively. Systematic deviation above the identity line indicates underconfidence; systematic deviation below indicates overconfidence.

3.3 Resolution vs. Reliability Trade-Off

Following Murphy (1973), the Brier Score decomposes as:

BS = (1/N) Σₖ nₖ(f̄ₖ − ōₖ)² − (1/N) Σₖ nₖ(ōₖ − ō)² + ō(1 − ō)

where the first term is the Reliability component REL = (1/N)Σₖ nₖ(f̄ₖ − ōₖ)², the second term is the Resolution component RES = (1/N)Σₖ nₖ(ōₖ − ō)², and the third term is the Uncertainty component UNC = ō(1−ō). Here, K is the number of forecast probability bins, nₖ is the number of forecasts in bin k, f̄ₖ is the mean stated probability in bin k, ōₖ is the observed relative frequency of positive outcomes in bin k, and ō is the overall base rate of positive outcomes. Since BS = REL − RES + UNC, a forecaster minimises their Brier Score by minimising REL (improving reliability) and maximising RES (improving resolution), with UNC fixed by the task.

The trade-off arises because reducing REL often means pulling extreme forecasts toward the centre of the probability scale, which tends to also reduce RES by decreasing the range of forecast probabilities. A forecaster advised by an AI system that systematically suggests more moderate estimates will see REL improve and RES decline. Whether the net effect on BS is positive depends on whether the AI's moderation is warranted — whether the forecaster's original extreme estimates were genuinely miscalibrated (overconfident) or genuinely reflective of high-information situations.

3.4 The Collaborative Calibration Gain (CCG) Metric

The Collaborative Calibration Gain is defined as the reduction in Brier Score attributable to AI collaboration, normalised by the Uncertainty of the question set:

CCG = (BSₕ − BSₕₐ) / UNC

where BSₕ is the Brier Score of the human forecaster operating without AI assistance, BSₕₐ is the Brier Score of the same forecaster (or a matched forecaster) operating with AI assistance, and UNC = ō(1−ō) is the Uncertainty of the question set. Normalising by Uncertainty makes CCG comparable across question sets with different base rates, since a Brier Score improvement of 0.05 on a question set with UNC = 0.1 represents a much larger proportional gain than the same absolute improvement on a question set with UNC = 0.25.

CCG can be decomposed into its reliability and resolution components:

CCGᴿᴱᴸ = (RELₕ − RELₕₐ) / UNC CCGᴿᴱˢ = (RESₕₐ − RESₕ) / UNC

A positive CCGᴿᴱᴸ indicates that AI collaboration improved reliability (reduced overconfidence or underconfidence). A positive CCGᴿᴱˢ indicates that AI collaboration improved resolution (better discrimination between higher- and lower-probability events). The theoretical ideal is a positive CCG driven by positive contributions from both components. The mirror trap failure mode is operationally defined as a situation in which CCGᴿᴱᴸ > 0 but CCGᴿᴱˢ < 0, with |CCGᴿᴱˢ| > CCGᴿᴱᴸ — that is, the AI improves reliability but destroys resolution, producing a net negative CCG.

3.5 The Mirror Dynamic as a Closed-Loop Bayesian Updater

The mirror dynamic can be formalised as a discrete-time Bayesian updating process. Let p_t denote the forecaster's subjective probability of event X at time step t, and let q_t denote the AI's current probability estimate for X, derived from ensemble aggregation, base rate retrieval, and the forecaster's expressed history. The AI's feedback function generates a suggested update signal:

Δₐ(t) = α · (q_t − p_t) + β · σ_q(t)

where α is the feedback weight (the degree to which the AI's estimate is applied as a corrective pressure), q_t − p_t is the discrepancy between AI and human estimates, β is the uncertainty weight, and σ_q(t) is the AI's uncertainty about its own estimate, expressed as a standard deviation. The second term models the AI's communication of its own epistemic uncertainty — a well-designed system communicates not just a point estimate but a confidence interval.

The forecaster's updated probability is then:

p_{t+1} = p_t + γ · Δₐ(t) + ε_t

where γ ∈ [0,1] is the forecaster's responsiveness to AI feedback (an individual difference parameter), and ε_t is an idiosyncratic random updating term capturing the forecaster's own new information acquisition and cognitive noise. In the long run, as the loop converges, p_∞ approaches a weighted combination of the forecaster's initial prior and the AI's reference estimate, with weights determined by α, β, and γ. This formal structure makes several predictions testable: that forecasters with higher γ (higher AI responsiveness) will show larger CCG in environments where q_t is accurate, but larger calibration degradation in environments where q_t is biased; that optimal feedback frequency is determined by the ratio of the AI's update speed to the forecaster's own natural updating cycle; and that systems with high β (explicit uncertainty communication) will produce smaller mirror trap effects than systems that communicate only point estimates.

Chapter 4: Research Methodology

This dissertation employs a multi-method convergent design, integrating five methodological streams that address complementary dimensions of the research questions. The streams are not independent: findings from each inform interpretation of the others, and the adversarial collaborative method explicitly requires synthesis across the quantitative and qualitative evidence bases.

4.1 Quantitative: Panel Data Regression of Brier Scores

Design. A panel dataset was assembled covering 847 forecasters participating in a large-scale prediction platform over an 18-month period (January 2023 – June 2024). The platform hosts questions across three primary domains: geopolitical events (42% of question stock), macroeconomic indicators (33%), and technology adoption/milestone events (25%). During the study period, the platform introduced an optional AI assistance module — providing base rate retrieval, ensemble deviation alerts, and structured decomposition prompts — which was adopted by approximately 62% of active forecasters in a staggered rollout, creating natural experimental variation in treatment exposure.

Sample. The 847 forecasters in the analytical sample were selected by a minimum activity threshold of 15 resolved forecasts per quarter, ensuring sufficient data for individual-level Brier Score estimation. The sample comprised 61% self-identified as operating in professional forecasting roles (intelligence analysis, financial risk, public health), 29% in academic or research roles, and 10% in prediction market hobbyist communities. Gender data: 67% male, 28% female, 5% non-binary or not disclosed. The sample is not representative of the general population; it is a high-engagement, high-expertise convenience sample, which limits external validity but maximises statistical power for the core research questions.

Variables. The primary outcome variable is the individual quarterly Brier Score (BSᵢₜ), computed from all resolved forecasts made by forecaster i in quarter t. Primary predictors include: AI module adoption (binary indicator, coded 1 from first quarter of module use), AI feedback frequency (log-transformed number of AI interactions per resolved forecast), domain expertise self-rating (1–7 Likert), team composition (proportion of questions for which the forecaster participated in team deliberation), and an overconfidence index (OCI) computed as the mean deviation between stated probability and calibration-curve-implied probability, averaged across all forecasts in the prior quarter.

Statistical Model. The primary model is a two-way fixed effects panel regression with forecaster fixed effects and quarter fixed effects, estimated using within-transformation ordinary least squares:

BSᵢₜ = αᵢ + γₜ + β₁ · AIᵢₜ + β₂ · FreqAIᵢₜ + β₃ · AIᵢₜ × OCIᵢₜ + β₄ · Expertiseᵢₜ + εᵢₜ

The interaction term AIᵢₜ × OCIᵢₜ tests whether the effect of AI adoption is moderated by prior overconfidence — a key prediction of the Calibration Mirror model. The within-estimator controls for all time-invariant forecaster characteristics (including baseline ability), and the period fixed effects control for common time trends (e.g., changes in question difficulty, platform-wide learning). Standard errors are clustered at the forecaster level to account for serial dependence within forecasters.

Identification Strategy. The staggered rollout of the AI module provides variation in treatment timing. Identification relies on the assumption that forecasters who adopted the module earlier do not systematically differ from later adopters in time-varying ways that affect Brier Scores conditional on fixed effects — a parallel trends assumption evaluated via event-study plots in the pre-period.

4.2 Qualitative: Semi-Structured Interviews

Design. Twenty-four semi-structured interviews were conducted with professional forecasters at prediction markets (n=8), intelligence analysis agencies (n=9), and policy research institutes (n=7). Interviews lasted 55–90 minutes and were conducted via secure video call with written consent for audio recording and transcription. All participants are referred to by pseudonym.

Recruitment. Participants were recruited through professional networks and snowball sampling, with the initial contact list drawn from the Good Judgment Project alumni network, the Superforecasting Foundation, and direct outreach to intelligence analysis units known to use structured analytic techniques. Purposive variation was sought on domain, seniority (3–22 years of forecasting experience), gender, and AI module adoption status (12 adopters, 12 non-adopters).

Interview Protocol. The semi-structured protocol covered: (1) the participant's forecasting practice and self-assessment of calibration strengths and weaknesses; (2) their experience with AI-assisted tools, including what feedback they found useful, confusing, or counterproductive; (3) their beliefs about how AI feedback had changed — or would change — their probability assignment processes; and (4) their perceptions of AI effects on team dynamics and forecast diversity.

Analysis. Transcripts were analysed using reflexive thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006). The first author coded all transcripts independently, generating an initial codebook of 84 codes. Codes were then hierarchically organised into themes through iterative memo-writing and constant comparative analysis. A second coder independently coded six transcripts (25% of the sample); inter-rater reliability was assessed using Cohen's κ = 0.81, indicating strong agreement. Divergent codings were resolved through discussion.

Positionality. The researcher is a quantitative forecasting practitioner who adopted AI assistance tools during the period of study. This creates a potential confirmatory bias toward finding that AI assistance is beneficial. Reflexive strategies to counter this bias included actively soliciting critical perspectives from non-adopters, writing counter-memos that articulated alternative interpretations of the data, and presenting preliminary findings to a critical research group prior to finalising the thematic structure.

4.3 Ethnographic: Embedded Observation at Meridian Group

Design. A 14-week period of embedded participant observation was conducted inside a geopolitical forecasting unit designated "Meridian Group" (a pseudonym used to protect organisational confidentiality). Meridian Group is a boutique firm of 11 professional forecasters that produces probabilistic assessments for government and financial services clients. The primary researcher was granted access in the role of visiting scholar, attending all team meetings, forecast review sessions, and client briefings during the observation period. Field notes were written within four hours of each observation session, averaging 1,200 words per session for a total field note corpus of approximately 67,000 words.

Ethics. Informed consent was obtained from all team members. Clients were not directly observed; their materials were accessible only in redacted form for analytical purposes. The study was approved by the institutional review board under protocol number IRB-2023-DX-0447. All identifying details have been altered to prevent identification of the organisation or its personnel.

Analytical Approach. Field notes were analysed using Geertz's (1973) thick description methodology, with attention to the symbolic and interpretive dimensions of practice — not merely what forecasters did, but what their actions meant within the normative frame of the epistemic community. Ritual analysis, following Turner (1969), was applied to identify recurring ceremonial structures in forecast production (the "forecast ceremony," the "update ritual," the "resolution reckoning") that revealed implicit beliefs about epistemic authority, uncertainty, and the role of AI feedback within the group's self-understanding.

4.4 Superforecasting: Prospective Tournament Design

Design. A structured six-month forecasting tournament (January–June 2024) was organised with 60 binary questions distributed across three domains: geopolitical (20 questions, e.g., "Will the UN Security Council pass a ceasefire resolution on conflict Y by date Z?"), macroeconomic (20 questions, e.g., "Will the US Federal Reserve cut the policy rate at least once by June 2024?"), and technology (20 questions, e.g., "Will GPT-5-class models demonstrate performance above threshold T on benchmark B by date Z?"). Questions were selected to have resolution dates distributed across the six-month window, preventing end-of-study bunching.

Participants. Eighty forecasters were recruited and randomly assigned to one of two conditions: AI-assisted (n=40, with access to the BlueLotus V3 SLICDO module and NITE-PEI probabilistic interface) or control (n=40, with access to standard base rate lookup tools but no AI feedback). Random assignment was stratified by prior Brier Score quartile (estimated from pre-tournament elicitation), domain expertise rating, and gender. Participants received monetary incentives based on tournament performance (\(150 base payment + \)3 per percentile rank improvement over the control group median).

AI System. The BlueLotus V3 system used by the AI-assisted group provides three primary feedback functions: (a) SLICDO (Structured Likelihood Integration with Calibration Deviation Output) — a module that computes the individual forecaster's expressed probability in the context of the ensemble distribution and flags estimates that are more than 1.5 standard deviations from the ensemble mean, with a breakdown of which reference classes support the forecaster's estimate and which do not; (b) NITE-PEI (Non-Iterative Temporal Elicitation with Probabilistic Evidence Integration) — a module that prompts the forecaster with structured decomposition questions (What base rate does this question fall under? What updating evidence has emerged since the base rate was established? How should this evidence shift the base rate? What residual uncertainty remains?) and integrates responses into a suggested probability estimate; (c) calibration curve visualisation — a real-time display of the forecaster's cumulative calibration curve based on their resolved forecasts.

Procedure. Both groups logged onto the platform for a minimum of three updating sessions per week. The AI-assisted group received SLICDO and NITE-PEI outputs automatically upon submitting a forecast or update. Both groups received resolution notifications and were able to review resolved questions. The primary outcome is the individual Brier Score at six-month resolution; secondary outcomes include updating frequency, forecast revision magnitude, and calibration curve slope.

4.5 Adversarial Collaborative: Structured Debate on AI Calibration Effects

Design. Following the adversarial collaboration protocol of Meehl (1978) and Kahneman and Klein (2009), a structured debate was organised between two teams of three researchers: Team Uplift, which defended the position that AI assistance systematically improves human calibration, and Team Anchor, which defended the position that AI assistance creates groupthink, anchoring bias, and the epistemic homogenisation of forecast distributions. Each team was given access to the same evidence base — the quantitative, qualitative, and ethnographic data streams from this dissertation — and was instructed to construct the strongest possible case for their assigned position, regardless of their personal views.

Protocol. The debate proceeded in four phases: (1) opening statement by each team (written, 3,000 words each); (2) written response to the opposing opening statement; (3) joint data re-analysis session in which both teams examined the evidence together and identified points of genuine empirical agreement and disagreement; (4) joint synthesis statement drafted collaboratively, identifying the conditions under which each position is supported.

Resolution Criterion. The synthesis was judged successful when all six participants agreed that the statement accurately represented both the strongest version of each position and the empirical conditions that differentiate them. This criterion was achieved after two rounds of revision to the joint synthesis statement.

5.2 Qualitative Findings: Thematic Analysis of Interviews

Three primary themes emerged from the thematic analysis of the 24 interview transcripts.

Theme 1: The AI as Epistemic Anchor. Twelve of the 24 participants described a recurring experience of arriving at a forecast question with a strong intuitive probability, encountering the AI's ensemble estimate, and experiencing a pull toward that estimate even before engaging with the reasoning the AI offered. Participant "Erica" (intelligence analysis, 11 years experience) described it as: "It's like the AI shows up and plants a flag in the probability space, and even though I know intellectually that I should evaluate its reasoning before I update, the number just sort of... settles into my head. I have to actively work against it to think freely." This anchoring dynamic was described as heightened when the AI's estimate was presented numerically (e.g., "0.63") rather than descriptively (e.g., "more likely than not"). Participants who had adopted the practice of writing their own initial estimate before viewing the AI's output reported less anchoring.

Theme 2: Resolution Enhancement Through Base Rate Integration. Nine participants described specific cases in which the AI's base rate retrieval had shifted their estimate in a direction they retrospectively judged as correct. Participant "Marcus" (prediction market, 7 years experience) offered a detailed account: "There was a question about whether a specific treaty would be ratified by a specific date. My intuition was around 30%, because I knew the political situation was complicated. The AI pulled up data showing that in the previous 40 analogous treaty negotiations, the ratification rate within that timeframe was 58%. I hadn't known that. I moved to 45%, and it resolved at yes. That kind of base rate anchoring — the good kind — is exactly what I want from an AI collaborator." This theme corresponds directly to the CCGᴿᴱˢ component — AI assistance improving resolution by providing information that helps forecasters distinguish more and less likely events.

Theme 3: Social Comparison and Identity. Seven participants, all operating in team forecasting contexts, described a social dimension to AI adoption that they found difficult to manage. Participant "Yolanda" (policy research institute, 14 years experience) articulated this most sharply: "When the AI flags that your forecast is an outlier — one-and-a-half standard deviations from the ensemble — there's a social pressure to conform that operates even when you believe you have genuinely distinctive information. The AI doesn't know that I just got off a call with a primary source. It only knows I'm outside the herd. And in a team environment, your colleagues can see the flag too. It becomes embarrassing to hold your position." This social mechanism creates a route to the mirror trap that operates independently of the individual cognitive mechanism: team norms of conformity, reinforced by AI flagging of outlier estimates, can suppress precisely the diverse viewpoints that the wisdom-of-crowds literature identifies as critical to ensemble accuracy.

5.3 Ethnographic Findings: Meridian Group

Overview of Epistemic Culture. Meridian Group exhibited a distinctive epistemic culture characterised by what the researcher came to term "calibrated humility" — a normative insistence on uncertainty quantification as a marker of professional seriousness, combined with a deep suspicion of overconfidence expressed by individual forecasters. This culture predated the introduction of AI tools and shaped the group's reception of AI feedback in significant ways.

Vignette 1: The Morning Calibration Ceremony. On a typical Tuesday in week three of the observation period, the team convened at 8:15 AM for what they called their "morning numbers" session. Each analyst briefly stated their current probability estimates on three active questions. The group leader, referred to as "Director Farris" throughout field notes, opened his laptop and pulled up the NITE-PEI interface. As the first analyst read her estimate of 0.72 for a question about parliamentary coalition formation, Director Farris immediately called up the ensemble deviation alert: "We're showing 0.72 against a SLICDO ensemble of 0.58. That's a significant deviation. What's your reasoning?" The analyst explained that she had seen a news report suggesting a key defector was returning to the coalition party. Director Farris paused, then typed the report into the evidence integration window. NITE-PEI recomputed and suggested 0.64. "Let's go with 0.66," Farris said — a compromise between the AI's recommendation and the analyst's informational advantage. The analyst recorded 0.66 in the system. This compromise pattern — "splitting the difference" between human insight and AI recommendation — recurred in 14 of the 23 morning sessions observed, suggesting a normative practice of moderating human-AI disagreement through arithmetic mediation rather than through deliberate reasoning about which source was more informative in the specific case.

Vignette 2: The Resolution Reckoning. In week nine, a question about commodity price movements that had been active for four months resolved. The true outcome was the event resolving at 0 (the price did not reach the threshold). The median team estimate at the time of resolution was 0.61 — a substantial forecasting error. Director Farris convened an unscheduled "resolution reckoning" session. "We had this at 61%. The SLICDO ensemble was at 58%. We were both wrong, but we were wrong together. What can we learn about where we both erred?" The team's discussion focused almost entirely on the base rates that had been retrieved — which turned out to have been drawn from an overlapping but not identical reference class. The failure was attributed to a reference class selection error, and a new protocol was established for verifying reference class boundaries. Notably, the team's own independent reasoning — the arguments individual analysts had made for their private estimates — was not discussed. The AI's reasoning trace had effectively crowded out the team's own deliberative post-mortem, substituting a focus on AI process errors for reflection on human reasoning errors.

Vignette 3: The Dissenting Analyst. In week twelve, one analyst — referred to as "Petra" — submitted a probability of 0.15 on a question where the SLICDO ensemble stood at 0.42. Director Farris raised the outlier flag in the morning session. Petra's reasoning was grounded in a private communication with a government official whose credibility she assessed as very high. The team debated for eighteen minutes. Four of the ten analysts explicitly stated that they were uncomfortable holding such a divergent position from the AI-generated ensemble. Two argued that if the AI had access to Petra's source, it would also move to around 0.15. The group eventually agreed to record 0.25 — again an arithmetic compromise, but this time one that Petra felt did not represent her genuine belief. "I don't feel like I got to make my real forecast," she told the researcher during a private conversation after the session. "I felt like I was managing the team's discomfort with uncertainty, not actually forecasting." The question resolved at 0.0 (the event did not occur), vindicating Petra's divergent estimate, but the team's recorded forecast was 0.25 — a substantially worse prediction than her suppressed view.

Ritual Analysis. These three vignettes reveal a ritual structure in Meridian Group's forecasting practice that serves to legitimate AI input as a form of epistemic authority, normalise arithmetic compromise between human and AI estimates as a decision procedure, and suppress outlier human views through social conformity pressure amplified by AI flagging. The effect is to create an epistemic culture in which the AI's probability estimates function not as informational inputs to individual Bayesian updating but as social facts whose deviation is marked and must be explained. This dynamic is the cultural embodiment of the mirror trap.

5.5 Adversarial Collaborative: Debate and Synthesis

Opening Positions. Team Uplift argued that the quantitative evidence (panel data and tournament results) provides strong causal warrant for concluding that AI assistance improves calibration. The effect persists across estimation strategies, is moderated in theoretically predicted ways (stronger for overconfident forecasters, non-linear in frequency), and is replicated across two methodologically independent designs. Team Anchor countered that the qualitative and ethnographic evidence reveals a mechanism — social conformity pressure amplified by AI flagging — that is genuinely harmful to epistemic independence and that the overall Brier Score improvement is purchased at the cost of forecast diversity. They pointed specifically to Petra's case: the suppression of a 0.15 estimate that proved correct in favour of a compromise 0.25 is a concrete demonstration of epistemic harm that is invisible in aggregate Brier Score statistics.

Joint Data Re-Analysis. The joint session revealed a genuine empirical disagreement: Team Uplift argued that Petra's case, while illustrative, is a single observation that cannot overturn the statistical evidence from 847 forecasters and 60 tournament questions. Team Anchor argued that statistical aggregation obscures the distribution of individual outcomes — that the mean CCG can be positive while a minority of forecasters experience CCG degradation, and that these minority experiences may disproportionately affect the most valuable component of forecast ensembles (the informed outliers). Both teams agreed, however, that this disagreement was empirically resolvable: by examining the CCG distribution at the individual level, one could determine what fraction of forecasters experienced positive versus negative CCG, and whether the negative CCG cases were concentrated among high-information forecasters.

Examination of the panel data at the individual level revealed that 73% of forecasters showed positive CCG, 15% showed CCG indistinguishable from zero, and 12% showed negative CCG. Critically, the negative-CCG group was disproportionately represented in the top quartile of prior Brier Score performance: 31% of top-quartile forecasters experienced negative CCG, compared to 8% of bottom-quartile forecasters. This finding provided the empirical foundation for the synthesis statement.

Synthesis Statement. The adversarial collaboration produced the following agreed synthesis: AI assistance produces robust mean improvement in calibration (positive CCG on average) but does so through a mechanism that selectively harms the most accurate human forecasters — those whose private information and distinctive perspectives would otherwise produce the forecast diversity that ensemble methods depend on for their superiority over individual predictions. The net effect on forecast quality depends on the ratio of mean CCG improvement to variance reduction harm: when the human-only ensemble is already well-diversified and contains well-calibrated high-information outliers, AI assistance may be net harmful; when the ensemble is dominated by systematic overconfidence and poor resolution, AI assistance is net beneficial. This synthesis position, which neither team held at the outset, represents a genuine advance over either initial position.

Chapter 6: Discussion

6.1 Theoretical Implications of the Calibration Mirror Model

The convergent evidence from five methodological streams provides robust empirical support for the core claims of the Calibration Mirror model. The closed-loop Bayesian framework correctly predicts: (a) that AI feedback produces calibration improvements that are larger for more overconfident forecasters; (b) that feedback frequency has a non-linear effect on calibration, with an optimal range that avoids both insufficient feedback (too little pull toward calibrated reference points) and excessive feedback (collapse of resolution through suppression of diverse estimates); and (c) that the mechanism operates through distinct reliability and resolution channels that can be independently tracked and optimised.

The formal parameter structure of the mirror dynamic model — specifically the γ parameter (individual responsiveness to AI feedback) and the β parameter (AI communication of uncertainty) — suggests a set of individual difference predictions that were not directly tested in this dissertation but merit investigation in future work. Specifically, the model predicts that forecasters with high γ (high responsiveness) will show greater benefit from AI assistance in environments where the AI is well-calibrated, but greater harm in environments where the AI is biased. Characterising the distribution of γ in professional forecasting populations, and understanding the cognitive and personality factors that predict high versus low γ, would be a valuable contribution to the practical literature on AI-assisted forecasting.

The formal expression for the AI feedback signal, Δₐ(t) = α(q_t − p_t) + β·σ_q(t), also has a direct design implication: AI systems that communicate only point estimates (β = 0) will produce stronger anchoring effects than systems that communicate uncertainty ranges. The ethnographic evidence from Meridian Group is consistent with this prediction: the SLICDO numerical deviation alert ("Your estimate is 0.72; the ensemble is 0.58") functions primarily as an anchoring device, while NITE-PEI's decomposition prompting functions more as a reasoning scaffold that preserves and potentially enhances individual epistemic independence.

6.2 The Mirror Trap Failure Mode

The mirror trap — defined operationally as a situation in which CCGᴿᴱᴸ > 0 but CCGᴿᴱˢ < 0, with |CCGᴿᴱˢ| > CCGᴿᴱᴸ — represents the most important failure mode identified in this research. Its mechanism, as evidenced across the quantitative, qualitative, and ethnographic data streams, involves a two-stage process. In the first stage, AI deviation flagging identifies individual forecasters whose estimates depart from the ensemble. In the second stage, social conformity pressure (in team contexts) or individual anchoring (in solo contexts) drives the forecaster's estimate toward the ensemble mean — reducing the variance of expressed probabilities across forecasters, which directly reduces the ensemble's Resolution component.

The mirror trap is particularly insidious because it is invisible in aggregate Brier Score statistics. When the forecaster with the distinctive, information-rich private estimate moves from 0.15 to 0.25, the team's aggregate Brier Score may actually improve in the short run if the majority of the team's estimates were already in the 0.40–0.60 range — the outlier correction pulls the ensemble mean toward the correct answer. But the long-run effect is to eliminate precisely the diversity of perspectives that makes ensemble forecasting superior to individual forecasting. An ensemble of 0.25-per-question forecasters, each having been privately calibrated to 0.15 by access to distinctive information, is a worse ensemble than one that preserves the full distribution.

The mirror trap failure mode has a direct analogue in Shannon's (1948) information theory. The information content of a probability distribution is measured by its entropy H(p) = −Σᵢ pᵢ log(pᵢ). When AI feedback collapses the distribution of expressed forecast probabilities, it reduces the entropy of the ensemble, which in information-theoretic terms represents a loss of information content. The mirror trap is, in this sense, an entropy reduction failure: the AI produces a lower-entropy ensemble that is less informative than the higher-entropy human-only ensemble it replaces, even when the lower-entropy ensemble has a lower mean Brier Score on average.

6.3 Optimal Feedback Latency

The panel regression finding that AI feedback frequency has a non-linear effect on Brier Score implies the existence of an optimal feedback latency — the time interval between forecast submission and AI feedback delivery that maximises CCG. The empirical evidence suggests this optimum lies in the range of one to three AI interactions per resolved forecast. Translating this into temporal terms depends on the mean resolution window of the question set: for questions with a six-month resolution window, one interaction per month appears approximately optimal; for questions with a two-week resolution window, one interaction per week.

The theoretical explanation for this optimum parallels insights from spaced repetition learning research (Ebbinghaus, 1885; Cepeda et al., 2006). Feedback that arrives too quickly after a forecast submission does not give the forecaster time to form independent judgments about new information before being influenced by the AI's updating signal. Feedback that arrives too slowly allows the forecaster's estimate to drift based on potentially miscalibrated private reasoning without the corrective influence of the AI reference point. The optimal interval matches the natural cycle of the forecaster's own updating — providing AI input at the moment when the forecaster is actively reconsidering their position, not before or long after.

The BlueLotus V3 system's NITE-PEI module implements a heuristic approximation of this optimal latency through an adaptive feedback scheduling algorithm that tracks the forecaster's own update frequency and delivers AI prompts at intervals that bracket the forecaster's natural reconsideration cycle. The empirical evidence from the tournament data is consistent with this design choice: forecasters using NITE-PEI showed the lowest rate of mirror trap incidence (3% of forecasters in the top Brier Score quartile experienced negative CCG, compared to 31% in the SLICDO-only group).

6.4 Domain Boundary Conditions

The tournament data's finding that AI assistance produces larger Brier Score improvements in the macroeconomic domain than in the technology domain reflects a general principle: AI assistance is most beneficial in domains where (a) large volumes of historical analogues exist for base rate calibration, (b) the relevant reference classes are well-defined and stable, and (c) the primary human calibration failure is overconfidence about low-base-rate events rather than information deficiency. In the macroeconomic domain, decades of institutional data on central bank behavior, GDP growth, and inflation dynamics provide a rich base rate substrate that SLICDO can retrieve effectively. In the technology domain, the novelty of AI capabilities and the absence of stable historical reference classes limit the AI's base rate advantage, leaving resolution improvement as the primary channel — which is more dependent on NITE-PEI's decomposition prompting and is less robust to the absence of analogical data.

This domain boundary analysis has direct implications for the deployment of AI forecasting assistance in intelligence analysis. Geopolitical forecasting combines elements of both favorable and unfavorable conditions: interstate conflict has extensive historical records, but the specific configurations of modern hybrid warfare, non-state actors, and information operations may not have adequate analogues. The finding that the geopolitical CCG (0.051 tournament Brier Score improvement) is intermediate between macroeconomic and technology suggests that domain heterogeneity within the geopolitical category — well-analogued sub-domains versus novel configurations — is averaging out to a moderate overall effect.

6.5 The MVR Principle Echo from Dissertation 1

This dissertation's findings resonate substantively with the Minimum Viable Representation (MVR) principle established in the first dissertation in this series (Dissertation-01). The MVR principle holds that the most epistemically productive representations are those that preserve enough complexity to capture decision-relevant variation without imposing cognitive overhead that impairs judgment. The present research applies an analogous principle to AI feedback design: optimal AI feedback is that which provides sufficient calibration signal to correct systematic errors without providing so much signal that it overwhelms or suppresses independent human judgment.

The mirror trap can be understood as a violation of the MVR principle in the temporal domain: excessive feedback frequency represents a "hyperrepresentation" of AI calibration signal that crowds out the human signal. The optimal feedback latency identified in Section 6.3 is, in effect, the temporal MVR point — the minimum feedback frequency required to achieve meaningful CCG without crossing into the mirror trap regime. This convergence between the theoretical frameworks of the two dissertations suggests that the MVR principle may function as a general constraint on productive human-AI epistemic collaboration that transcends specific application domains.

6.6 Limitations

Several limitations qualify the conclusions of this research. The panel dataset relies on platform-recorded forecasts; forecasters' private beliefs may diverge from their platform submissions, particularly in contexts where social visibility of forecasts creates conformity pressure. The ethnographic work is localised to a single organisation; Meridian Group's particular culture of "calibrated humility" may not generalise to forecasting organisations with different epistemic norms. The tournament was conducted over six months, which may be insufficient for detecting longer-run effects of AI assistance on forecaster development — specifically, whether AI assistance accelerates or retards the development of the underlying forecasting skills that produce calibration gains.

The formal model of the mirror dynamic assumes linearity in the updating function — a simplification that allows analytic tractability but may miss non-linear threshold effects (e.g., the possibility that AI feedback has no effect below a certain salience threshold and a large effect above it). The adversarial collaborative synthesis, while intellectually satisfying, reflects the views of a small group of researchers and should be treated as a hypothesis-generating synthesis rather than a definitive resolution of the Team Uplift vs. Team Anchor debate.

Chapter 7: Conclusion

7.1 Summary of Findings

This dissertation has investigated the Calibration Mirror dynamic — the closed-loop feedback relationship between human forecasters and AI calibration assistance systems — through five methodological lenses. The converging evidence supports a nuanced conclusion: human-AI collaborative forecasting produces substantial mean improvements in probabilistic accuracy, as measured by Brier Score and decomposed into reliability and resolution components, under conditions of moderate feedback latency and appropriate domain coverage. The Collaborative Calibration Gain metric (CCG = (BSₕ − BSₕₐ)/UNC) provides a principled, decomposable measure of this improvement.

However, the same evidence base documents a genuine failure mode — the mirror trap — in which excessive AI feedback frequency, amplified by social conformity norms in team contexts, collapses the diversity of expressed probability distributions and suppresses the high-information outlier forecasts that are epistemically most valuable. The mirror trap is an entropy-reduction failure in the information-theoretic sense: the AI produces a more homogeneous ensemble with lower mean error but reduced capacity to represent the full distribution of available evidence. The adversarial collaborative synthesis identifies the conditions that separate beneficial from harmful AI assistance: the ratio of systematic overconfidence in the human ensemble (which AI reliably corrects) to diversity of private information (which AI risks suppressing) is the key determinant of net CCG sign.

The BlueLotus V3 SLICDO and NITE-PEI modules instantiate complementary aspects of the theoretical framework: SLICDO addresses the reliability channel by flagging deviation from calibrated ensembles, while NITE-PEI addresses the resolution channel by scaffolding decomposition reasoning that preserves and extends individual epistemic independence. The tournament data confirms that their combined use produces superior calibration outcomes to either module alone.

7.2 Policy Recommendations for AI Deployment in Forecasting

Based on the theoretical framework and empirical evidence developed in this dissertation, seven policy recommendations are offered for institutions considering deploying AI assistance in professional forecasting contexts:

Recommendation 1 — Decomposition-First, Deviation-Second. AI feedback systems should prioritise resolution enhancement (NITE-PEI-style decomposition prompting) over reliability correction (SLICDO-style deviation flagging). The mirror trap risk is disproportionately associated with deviation flagging, which operates through social conformity pressure; decomposition prompting preserves epistemic independence while improving information integration.

Recommendation 2 — Protect Outlier Estimates. Organisations should establish explicit normative protections for forecasters who hold well-reasoned outlier positions. A formal protocol analogous to Meridian Group's post-hoc resolution reckoning should be extended to the pre-resolution period: when a forecaster's estimate deviates significantly from the AI ensemble, the team should inquire whether the forecaster has private information before applying conformity pressure.

Recommendation 3 — Calibrate Feedback Latency to Question Resolution Window. AI feedback frequency should be matched to the natural reconsideration cycle of the question set, targeted at one to three interactions per resolved forecast. Automated adaptive scheduling (as implemented in NITE-PEI) is preferable to fixed-frequency feedback.

Recommendation 4 — Communicate Uncertainty, Not Just Point Estimates. AI systems should always communicate the uncertainty of their own estimates (the σ_q(t) term in the formal model) alongside the point estimate. This reduces the anchoring effect and supports more nearly Bayesian updating by forecasters.

Recommendation 5 — Track CCG Decomposition in Institutional Reporting. Institutions should monitor not only aggregate Brier Score improvement but also the reliability and resolution components separately. A drop in CCGᴿᴱˢ with sustained CCGᴿᴱᴸ is an early warning signal of mirror trap onset.

Recommendation 6 — Preserve Forecast Diversity as an Institutional Asset. Ensemble forecast quality depends on the diversity of expressed probabilities across forecasters. Institutions should monitor inter-forecaster variance as a quality indicator distinct from mean accuracy, and should investigate sharp drops in variance as potential evidence of AI-induced epistemic homogenisation.

Recommendation 7 — Domain-Match AI Assistance Intensity to Base Rate Availability. AI assistance intensity should be calibrated to the richness of available historical base rate data for the specific forecasting domain. In data-rich macroeconomic and institutional domains, SLICDO deviation flagging is highly effective. In data-sparse technology and geopolitical novelty domains, NITE-PEI decomposition prompting is the more reliable tool.

7.3 Future Research Directions

Several important questions remain open. First, the long-run effect of AI assistance on forecaster skill development is unknown. If AI feedback functions as a scaffolding that gradually internalises calibration norms in the human forecaster's own reasoning, then CCG might increase over time even at constant AI assistance intensity. Conversely, if AI assistance creates cognitive dependency — reducing the forecaster's motivation to develop their own base rate knowledge — then CCG might decline as the novelty of AI feedback diminishes. A longitudinal extension of the panel design, tracking forecasters over three to five years with and without AI assistance, would address this question.

Second, the formal model of the mirror dynamic requires empirical parameter estimation. Specifically, estimating the individual-difference parameters α (AI feedback weight), β (uncertainty communication weight), and γ (forecaster responsiveness) from behavioral data would enable the model to generate precise quantitative predictions that could be tested prospectively. Bayesian methods for estimating these parameters from individual-level forecasting histories exist in the psychometric literature and would be directly applicable.

Third, the relationship between AI calibration assistance and epistemic autonomy — the ability of forecasters to maintain and express their genuine subjective beliefs — deserves dedicated investigation. The ethnographic evidence suggests that AI assistance may create a form of epistemic dependency analogous to the overcorrection effects documented in cognitive prosthetics research: individuals who rely on external cognitive supports may lose the ability to perform unaided when the support is removed. Experimental designs that withdraw AI assistance after extended exposure would test this hypothesis.

Fourth, the adversarial collaborative method used in this dissertation proved productive in ways that were not fully anticipated. The joint synthesis statement identified an empirically resolvable disagreement — the distribution of individual CCG — that neither team's initial position addressed. Developing a more formal protocol for adversarial collaboration in forecasting research, with explicit rules for evidence adjudication and synthesis criteria, would be a methodological contribution with broad applicability.
