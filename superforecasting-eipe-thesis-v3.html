Chapter I

Introduction

Financial markets are prediction engines. Tetlock's Expert Political Judgment (2005) found many experts near chance — with an inverse correlation between media fame and accuracy. Yet IARPA's Good Judgment Project identified a thin tail of superforecasters: fox-like thinkers who update frequently, decompose questions, and score well on the Brier rule (Mellers et al., 2015).

BlueLotus asks a sharper question than Research Commission 001:

Can we build our own superforecasting institution — measuring our probabilities against our resolutions until Brier forces honesty?

— BlueLotus V3 design session, June 2026

Answer: We are building it. It is not mature yet. The architecture is correct. The discipline is the moat.

Chapter II

Literature Review: What Works and What Does Not

What works (evidence)

  • Strictly proper scoring — Brier rewards honest probabilities (Gneiting & Raftery, 2007)
  • Murphy decomposition — separate reliability, resolution, uncertainty
  • Fox thinking — many lenses; hedgehogs lose (Tetlock, 2005)
  • Base rates + updating — outside view first; frequent belief revision
  • Elite teams + debiasing — superforecasters partly created (Mellers et al., 2015)
  • Clairvoyance test — binary, time-bounded, pre-registered resolution criteria

What fails (evidence)

  • Famous pundits — inverse fame-accuracy (Tetlock & Gardner, 2015)
  • Hedgehog certainty — one grand theory filters all evidence
  • Narrative without numbers — unscored commentary
  • ML / regression on price — backtest overfitting (Bailey et al., 2014)
  • OOS return models — rarely beat baselines (Goyal & Welch, 2008)
  • Promoting before resolution — false confidence before 90d maturity
Murphy Decomposition
BS = Reliability − Resolution + Uncertainty
Fix Reliability first (calibration). Resolution improves with better event selection. Uncertainty is the irreducible noise floor.
