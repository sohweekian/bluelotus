Abstract

This thesis presents the BlueLotus Geopolitical Game Theory Model (BGTM-V1): a formal, mathematically rigorous framework for analysing multi-player geopolitical situations using N-player Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE), Quantal Response Equilibrium (QRE) calibration, and Correlated Equilibrium multiplicity resolution, integrated directly into a live autonomous financial intelligence pipeline.

The thesis makes five original contributions to the intersection of computational game theory, geopolitical risk modelling, and quantitative portfolio management. BGTM-V1 supports up to 10 players, expert-initialised and Brier-calibrated payoff tensors, and an outcome space of up to 100 terminal market states.

Two live case studies are presented: the Strait of Hormuz Standoff (2026) modelled as a seven-player signalling game, and the BOJ-Fed-ECB Rate Policy Game (2026) as a four-player Stackelberg sequential game, demonstrating the model's operation on real-time intelligence data with direct portfolio implications.

BGTM-V1 fills the empty geopolitical_overlay layer of the BlueLotus V3 system, upgrading qualitative narrative analysis to a formal, calibrated, Bayesian-integrated strategic intelligence layer. The model does not replace human judgment — it provides the CIO with a structurally rigorous advisory framework that quantifies the strategic logic underlying geopolitical events.

Chapter 1

Introduction

1.1 The Problem of Geopolitical Intelligence in Quantitative Finance

Modern quantitative financial intelligence systems face a structural gap at the boundary between mathematical rigour and geopolitical reality. Portfolio models can precisely compute Value at Risk, calibrate volatility surfaces, and optimise Kelly fractions to four decimal places. Yet when a military commander issues a threat to close a critical shipping strait, or a central bank governor signals a historic policy reversal, the same systems fall silent — or worse, reduce these events to a binary input in a lookup table.

This gap is not merely aesthetic. Geopolitical events are not independent shocks. They are the observable outputs of strategic interactions between rational (and boundedly rational) actors with defined objectives, private information, and interdependent payoff structures. They are, in the precise sense used by John Nash in 1951, the outcomes of non-cooperative games.

The failure to model them as such has material consequences. A portfolio system that reads "Iran issues Hormuz closure warning" and increments an oil price risk scalar by 15% is doing something fundamentally different from a system that asks: what is the Nash Equilibrium of the multi-player game Iran is currently playing, and what is the probability that the closure threat is a credible commitment versus cheap talk?

"In strategic situations, the outcome depends not merely on what you do, but on what you believe others will do, and what they believe you will do. The equilibrium is the fixed point of these mutual beliefs."

— John F. Nash Jr. (1950). Equilibrium Points in N-Person Games. PNAS.

1.2 The BlueLotus V3 Context

The BlueLotus V3 system is a 65-step autonomous financial intelligence pipeline running every 39 minutes on local hardware. It ingests signals from 16 data sources, runs four analytical layers (ACMS, NITE-PEI, Kelly Sizing, Governance Gate), generates multi-format reports, and publishes live intelligence to a public dashboard under strict safety doctrines: no autonomous order generation, no broker route access, and mandatory human CIO execution authority.

The pipeline contains an explicit geopolitical_overlay field in its data schema. As of this writing, that field is empty — populated with {} — across all pipeline cycles. This is the architectural gap that BGTM-V1 is designed to fill.

Chapter 2

Literature Review

2.1 Nash (1950, 1951) — Foundations

John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern laid the formal foundations in Theory of Games and Economic Behavior (1944), addressing zero-sum two-player games via the minimax theorem. The decisive extension to N-player non-cooperative games came from John F. Nash Jr. in two landmark papers — Equilibrium Points in N-Person Games (PNAS, 1950) and Non-Cooperative Games (Annals of Mathematics, 1951). Nash proved the existence of at least one equilibrium point in every finite N-player non-cooperative game using Kakutani's fixed-point theorem applied to the best-response correspondence. This existence result is BGTM-V1's foundational guarantee: the solver always returns at least one solution.

2.2 Harsanyi (1967–68) — Incomplete Information

Classical Nash Equilibrium assumes complete information — each player knows all payoff functions. This assumption fails catastrophically in geopolitics. John Harsanyi solved this in his trilogy Games with Incomplete Information Played by "Bayesian" Players (Management Science, 1967–68). Harsanyi introduced types — private information encoding each player's true payoffs, capabilities, and resolve. Players share a common prior distribution over types. The solution concept is Bayesian Nash Equilibrium (BNE): each player maximises expected utility given their type and Bayesian beliefs about others' types. BGTM-V1 adopts BNE as its primary solution concept.

2.3 Selten (1965, 1975) and Kreps-Wilson (1982) — Refinements

Multiple Nash Equilibria are the norm, not the exception. Reinhard Selten's Subgame Perfect Equilibrium (1965) and Trembling Hand Perfect Equilibrium (1975) eliminate implausible equilibria based on incredible threats and off-path irrationality. Kreps and Wilson's Sequential Equilibrium (Econometrica, 1982) extends this to extensive form games with a consistency requirement on beliefs. BGTM-V1 applies Trembling Hand Perfection for normal form games and Sequential Equilibrium for sequential move games.

2.4 McKelvey-Palfrey (1995) — Quantal Response Equilibrium

Perfect rationality is false in practice. McKelvey and Palfrey's Quantal Response Equilibrium (Games and Economic Behavior, 1995) models bounded rationality through the logit choice function:

QRE Logit Specification
σᵢᴼᴿᴱ(sᵢ | tᵢ) = exp(λ · EUᵢ(sᵢ, σ₋ᵢ, tᵢ)) / Σ_{s'ᵢ} exp(λ · EUᵢ(s'ᵢ, σ₋ᵢ, tᵢ))

The parameter λ ≥ 0 is the rationality parameter: λ = 0 gives uniform randomisation; λ → ∞ converges to Nash Equilibrium. BGTM-V1 calibrates λ per game class via Brier scoring of historical player move predictions — transforming the rationality assumption from a fixed axiom into a measurable, empirically grounded quantity.

2.5 Aumann (1974, 1987) — Correlated Equilibrium

Robert Aumann's Correlated Equilibrium generalises Nash Equilibrium by allowing player strategies to be correlated through a shared signal or mediator. Every Nash Equilibrium is a Correlated Equilibrium, but CE is strictly larger — it encompasses convex combinations of NE and additional correlated strategy profiles. BGTM-V1 uses CE as the multiplicity resolution mechanism: when multiple Nash Equilibria survive the refinement cascade, the maximum social payoff CE provides a principled, unique probability distribution over outcomes.

2.6 Carlsson-Van Damme (1993) and Morris-Shin (2002) — Global Games

Carlsson and Van Damme's Global Games and Equilibrium Selection (Econometrica, 1993) showed that adding small noise to players' information about underlying fundamentals yields a unique equilibrium in coordination games that would otherwise have multiple equilibria. Morris and Shin (2002) extended this to banking crises, currency attacks, and speculative positions — all threshold coordination games. BGTM-V1 uses the global games framework for kill condition threshold integration: the WATCH → TRIGGERED transition corresponds to a global games threshold crossing, giving a unique, payoff-derived trigger probability.

2.7 Crawford-Sobel (1982) — Signalling and Cheap Talk

Crawford and Sobel's Strategic Information Transmission (Econometrica, 1982) formalised cheap talk games in which a Sender with private information communicates with a Receiver at zero cost. The central result: equilibrium communication is partition-based — the Sender reveals which interval of the state space the true value falls in, not the precise value. The number of intervals is bounded by the conflict of interest between Sender and Receiver. This framework directly applies to Iran's military/diplomatic signalling dynamic: the military signal (high conflict of interest) is nearly uninformative; the diplomatic signal (low conflict of interest) carries real information.

2.8 Fearon (1995) and Bueno de Mesquita (1981) — Geopolitical Applications

James Fearon's Rationalist Explanations for War (International Organization, 1995) identified private information, commitment problems, and issue indivisibility as the three rationalist causes of conflict — providing the game-theoretic foundation for analysing Iran's closure threat credibility. Bruce Bueno de Mesquita's The War Trap (1981) quantified expected utility calculations for conflict initiation. Robert Powell's Nuclear Deterrence Theory (1990) formalised deterrence as a sequential game of incomplete information — directly applicable to the US-Iran Hormuz dynamic.

3.2 Bayesian Nash Equilibrium

A behavioural strategy σᵢ: Tᵢ → Δ(Sᵢ) maps each type to a mixed strategy. The profile σ* is a Bayesian Nash Equilibrium if for every player i, every type tᵢ:

BNE Condition
EUᵢ(σᵢ*, σ₋ᵢ* | tᵢ) ≥ EUᵢ(σᵢ', σ₋ᵢ* | tᵢ) ∀ σᵢ' ∈ ℬᵢ

Existence guarantee: By Nash (1951) and Harsanyi (1967), every finite BGTM-V1 game has at least one BNE in mixed strategies. The solver always returns a non-empty output.

3.3 The 10-Player, 100-Outcome Design

Ten players covers every geopolitical situation of interest: the Hormuz standoff involves 7 actors, and adding observer states (IAEA, EU, Russia) reaches the ceiling. For 10 players with binary strategies, the pure strategy space is 2¹⁰ = 1,024 profiles. With 3 strategies each: 3¹⁰ = 59,049. After Iterated Elimination of Dominated Strategies (IEDS) pre-processing, the effective space reduces by 60–80%, yielding tractable computation within a 39-minute pipeline cycle.

One hundred outcome slots cover the full branching space of complex multi-theatre scenarios while maintaining a tractable representation. Each outcome carries market-relevant implications: oil price delta, gold price delta, SPY/VXX directional signals, and thesis probability update deltas for all active NITE-PEI theses.

Chapter 4

Solution Concepts and the Refinement Cascade

BGTM-V1 applies solution concepts in a deterministic cascade from strongest to weakest:

# Solution Concept Cascade
Step 1  Dominant Strategy Equilibrium (DSE)
        — Strongest. If each player has a dominant strategy, unique solution.

Step 2  Iterated Elimination of Dominated Strategies (IEDS)
        — Reduces strategy space. If unique solution remains, done.

Step 3  Pure Strategy Nash Equilibrium
        — One or more NE in pure strategies.

Step 4  Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium
        — Guaranteed to exist (Nash 1951).

Step 5  Trembling Hand Perfect Equilibrium
        — Eliminates NE based on implausible off-path strategies.

Step 6  Sequential Equilibrium  [extensive form games only]
        — Eliminates NE inconsistent with sequential rationality (Kreps-Wilson).

Step 7  Correlated Equilibrium Envelope  [if multiple NE remain]
        — Probability-weighted combination of all valid NE.

Step 8  QRE Layer
        — Applies bounded rationality correction (λ calibrated per game class).

The Correlated Equilibrium LP

When multiple NE survive the cascade, the CE solves a linear programme to find the probability distribution ρ* over action profiles that maximises expected total payoff subject to incentive compatibility for all players. This is solvable in polynomial time (Papadimitriou-Roughgarden, 2008) and always has a feasible solution (every NE is a feasible point).

Correlated Equilibrium LP
max_ρ Σᵢ Σ_s ρ(s) · uᵢ(s) s.t. Σ_{s₋ᵢ} ρ(sᵢ, s₋ᵢ) · [uᵢ(sᵢ, s₋ᵢ) − uᵢ(s'ᵢ, s₋ᵢ)] ≥ 0 ∀ i ∈ N, sᵢ ∈ Sᵢ, s'ᵢ ∈ Sᵢ
Chapter 5

The Geo-LR Bridge

The NITE-PEI Bayesian engine operates on Likelihood Ratios. The Geo-LR Bridge translates Nash equilibrium outcome probabilities into LRs for direct consumption:

Dynamic Geo-LR Formula
LR_geo(c | θⱼ) = P(ωₖ consistent with θⱼ | event c observed) / P(ωₖ consistent with θⱼ | no event observed)

When player i makes move sᵢ (observed as event class c), the posterior over game outcomes is updated via Bayes' rule using the CE solution ρ*. This posterior is then converted to a thesis-level LR and fed into the NITE-PEI Bayesian updater.

LR Blending During Cold Start

During cold start (calibration_n < 30), the dynamic Geo-LR is blended with the static NITE-PEI LR to prevent replacement of a tested value with an uncalibrated one:

Blended LR
LR_blend = (1 − α) · LR_static + α · LR_geo α = min(1.0, calibration_n / 30)

The blend shifts entirely to the dynamic Geo-LR as calibration accumulates. At calibration_n = 0, the system behaves identically to the pre-BGTM-V1 pipeline — full backward compatibility.

QRE-Brier Calibration Methodology

The QRE rationality parameter λ is not a theoretical constant — it is a measurable property of specific geopolitical actor classes in specific strategic environments. The QRE-Brier methodology estimates λ empirically:

Player Move Brier Scoring

At each pipeline cycle, BGTM-V1 records: (1) the QRE predicted move probability distribution; (2) the observed player move extracted from news signals; (3) the Brier score for the prediction. Lower Brier scores indicate better calibration. The Brier score is proper — minimising expected Brier score is equivalent to maximising calibration.

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Log-Likelihood of λ
ℓ(λ) = Σ_t Σᵢ log σᵢᴼᴿᴱ(sᵢ,ₜᵒᵇˢ | λ) λ̂ = argmax_{λ≥0} ℓ(λ)

Chapter 10

Limitations and Honest Assessment

10.1 The Payoff Specification Problem

The most significant limitation is that payoff matrices are unobservable. We cannot directly measure what Iran values or the precise resolve threshold of the US Navy. Payoff matrices are expert estimates — structured, auditable, and systematically updated via revealed preference, but ultimately subjective. Mitigations: explicit uncertainty (payoffs are random variables with specified variance), revealed preference updating (observed moves constrain payoffs), sensitivity analysis over payoff ranges, and calibration_n tracking (the model honestly reports its confidence stage).

10.2 Rational Actor Assumption

QRE addresses bounded rationality at the individual player level. But some events are driven by domestic political irrationality, black swans, or model specification error — forces the game structure does not capture. The correct epistemic posture: BGTM-V1 is a structured reasoning tool, not an oracle. It forces explicit specification of assumptions and enables systematic updating. It is superior to informal narrative analysis because it makes assumptions auditable and logic reproducible. It is not superior to human judgment in ways that cannot be made explicit.

10.3 Cold Start Period

At deployment, all games have calibration_n = 0. The blended LR α = 0, meaning the system behaves identically to the pre-BGTM-V1 pipeline during cold start. The system degrades gracefully — it never substitutes an uncalibrated dynamic LR for a tested static one. The transition from static to dynamic is smooth and monitored.
