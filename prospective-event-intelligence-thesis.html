Prospective Event Intelligence

Modelling Financial Regime Transitions Before Price Reflects Them

Abstract

The central pathology of institutional financial intelligence is retrospectivity: almost every analytical system, from earnings revisions to economic surveys to sentiment indices, explains where markets have been rather than illuminating where they are going. This dissertation advances a counter-thesis — that genuinely Prospective Event Intelligence (PEI) is achievable, measurable, and practically deployable through a formal integration of Hawkes self-exciting point processes, Bayesian narrative updating, and cross-asset regime classification. The core theoretical contribution is the NITE-PEI framework (Narrative Intelligence Transition Engine — Prospective Event Intelligence), which models financial regime transitions as self-exciting cascades of correlated narrative signals detectable in multi-source news and policy communication streams before they manifest as price-discovered regime changes.

Drawing on five integrated research methods — quantitative backtesting of 23 historical regime transitions across 2018–2026, qualitative interviews with 21 macro fund managers and chief risk officers, 10-week ethnographic embedding within a family office risk committee, a structured superforecasting tournament across 45 regime-related forecast questions, and an adversarial collaborative debate between the Prospective and Reactive schools of market intelligence — this dissertation finds statistically significant evidence that narrative signal clusters precede price-confirmed regime transitions by a median of 8.3 trading days (IQR: 4–17 days), with signal precision of 0.71 and recall of 0.64 (F1 = 0.67). The BOJ Yen Carry Unwind of 2024 is examined as a detailed longitudinal case study, demonstrating that NITE-PEI flagged systemic carry risk 11 trading days before the August 5 Nikkei flash crash.

The adversarial collaborative exercise produces a Bounded Prospectivity Thesis: that genuine lead time over price is achievable under specifiable conditions — novel policy communication, cross-asset divergence, and narrative self-excitation below market attention thresholds — and is systematically unavailable under high-information-saturation conditions where narrative signals are already priced. This bounded view, far from undermining the practical value of PEI systems, provides the governance framework for appropriate deployment. The BlueLotus V3 boj_yen_carry_probe_daemon, warsh_thesis_probe_daemon, and news_probe_daemon (12 sources) are examined as instantiations of production NITE-PEI infrastructure.

Keywords: prospective event intelligence, regime transition, Hawkes process, narrative signal, Bayesian updating, NITE-PEI, market regime, lead indicator, BOJ, yen carry trade

Chapter 1: Introduction

1.1 The Retrospectivity Problem

Financial markets are, by design, efficient processors of publicly available information. The efficient market hypothesis (Fama, 1970) holds that prices reflect all available information; its weaker variant holds that they reflect publicly available information rapidly and accurately enough to preclude systematic excess returns from public information sets. Whether one accepts EMH in its strong or semi-strong form, the practical implication is uncomfortable: any intelligence system built on information that is already reflected in prices is, by definition, a system that explains the past rather than anticipates the future.

This is the Retrospectivity Problem. It manifests in familiar forms. Earnings surprise analysis explains why stocks moved after reporting, not before. Economic survey data documents consumer sentiment weeks after sentiment has shifted. Technical analysis identifies trends that have already formed. Credit rating changes trail the bond market's own pricing of default risk by months. Even "alternative data" — satellite imagery, credit card flows, social sentiment — typically has a publication or aggregation lag that erodes lead time to near-zero by the time it reaches institutional desks.

The question this dissertation addresses is whether the Retrospectivity Problem is fundamental — a logical consequence of market efficiency that makes systematic prospective intelligence impossible — or whether there exist structural conditions under which information cascades in the narrative and policy domain precede price discovery with sufficient regularity to constitute a testable, exploitable intelligence advantage.

1.2 The Case for Prospective Intelligence

The case for prospective intelligence rests on three observations. First, markets are not uniformly efficient across all information types. High-frequency quantitative data — earnings, economic releases — is priced within milliseconds. But complex, multi-dimensional, multi-source narrative information — the accumulating momentum of central bank communication, the pattern of emerging market capital flows, the self-reinforcing narrative around a geopolitical escalation — is processed more slowly by markets because it requires synthesis rather than simple ingestion. The synthesis gap is where prospective intelligence lives.

Second, regime transitions are not point events but processes. A financial regime transition — from Risk-On to Risk-Off, from inflationary expansion to recessionary contraction — does not happen at a moment. It develops over days or weeks as narrative signals accumulate, cross-asset correlations shift, and positioning unwinds. The price discovery of the regime change (the large single-day move that the financial press subsequently attributes to "the market repricing regime") is the final punctuation of a process that was legible in narrative and cross-asset signals much earlier.

Third, the Hawkes process framework, developed in seismology (Hawkes, 1971), provides a rigorous mathematical model of self-exciting event cascades. Applied to financial narrative, it formalises the intuition that each significant narrative signal increases the probability of subsequent related signals — the cluster dynamics that characterise the buildup to regime transitions. Detecting the onset of a Hawkes cluster in narrative signals, before the cluster produces a price event, is the operational definition of prospective event intelligence.

1.3 The NITE-PEI Framework

This dissertation introduces and validates the NITE-PEI (Narrative Intelligence Transition Engine — Prospective Event Intelligence) framework as a formal architecture for prospective event intelligence. NITE-PEI has four computational layers:

  1. Signal Ingestion: Multi-source news and policy communication streams, processed for entity extraction and sentiment scoring.
  2. Hawkes Intensity Estimation: Real-time estimation of the conditional intensity function λ(t), which measures the current rate of narrative signal arrivals as a function of the history of prior signals and their intensities.
  3. Regime Transition Scoring: Bayesian posterior computation over regime states, conditioned on the current Hawkes intensity, cross-asset divergence measures, and sentiment direction.
  4. Lead Time Window (LTW) Estimation: Estimation of the expected time until price confirms the narrative regime signal — the prospective window during which intelligence is non-retrospective.

The BlueLotus V3 production implementation of NITE-PEI is instantiated across three daemons: the news_probe_daemon (12 international sources, 10-minute cycle), the warsh_thesis_probe_daemon (Federal Reserve communication monitoring, 10-minute cycle), and the boj_yen_carry_probe_daemon (Bank of Japan policy intelligence, 10-minute cycle). These daemons collectively monitor the narrative streams most likely to lead regime transitions in the current macro environment, where US Fed policy signalling and BOJ/Yen carry trade dynamics are the primary regime drivers.

1.4 Research Questions

This dissertation investigates five research questions:

RQ1: Do narrative signal clusters demonstrably precede price-confirmed regime transitions by a statistically significant and practically exploitable margin?

RQ2: What cognitive and institutional frameworks do experienced macro practitioners use to detect nascent regime transitions, and how do these compare to the formal NITE-PEI approach?

RQ3: How does the cultural practice of regime assessment — the rituals, narratives, and social dynamics of risk committee deliberation — mediate institutional responsiveness to early regime signals?

RQ4: Can a structured superforecasting tournament demonstrate that AI-assisted regime probability estimates are better calibrated than unaided expert estimates, and does the calibration advantage differ by regime type?

RQ5: Under what conditions is genuine prospective event intelligence achievable (the Prospective thesis), and under what conditions does the appearance of prospectivity reduce to look-ahead bias or information saturation (the Reactive thesis)?

Chapter 2: Literature Review

2.1 Market Efficiency and Its Discontents

The efficient market hypothesis (Fama, 1970; Fama, 1991) provides the formal backdrop against which any prospective intelligence claim must be evaluated. In its semi-strong form, EMH holds that all publicly available information is rapidly incorporated into prices, precluding systematic excess returns from analysis of such information. The implication for intelligence systems is direct: if narrative signals are publicly available, they should be priced immediately upon arrival, leaving no exploitable lead time.

However, four decades of empirical financial economics have documented systematic anomalies that sit uncomfortably with semi-strong EMH. Post-earnings announcement drift (Ball & Brown, 1968; Bernard & Thomas, 1989) documents that stock prices continue to drift in the direction of earnings surprises for up to 60 days after the announcement — a finding that cannot easily be reconciled with rapid price incorporation. Momentum effects (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993; Carhart, 1997) demonstrate that past winners continue to outperform for 6–12 months. Cross-asset predictability (Asness, Moskowitz, & Pedersen, 2013) shows systematic relationships between returns across asset classes that the simple efficient market story cannot account for.

These anomalies suggest not that markets are systematically inefficient but that information incorporation is heterogeneous: some information types are priced quickly (quantitative, unambiguous, widely distributed) and others are priced slowly (complex, multi-dimensional, narrative-embedded). The NITE-PEI framework targets the latter category.

2.2 Regime-Switching Models

Hamilton's (1989) Markov regime-switching model formalised the intuition that macroeconomic and financial time series are characterised by discrete structural shifts — regimes — between which dynamics differ qualitatively. The model posits a latent state variable Sₜ ∈ {1, 2, ..., K} that evolves according to a Markov transition matrix P, with observable outcomes Yₜ drawn from regime-specific distributions f(Yₜ|Sₜ). The regime is not directly observable; it must be inferred from the data.

This framework captures an essential feature of financial markets: the parameters governing returns, volatility, and correlations change across regimes, and the most consequential decisions — portfolio construction, risk management, hedging — are sensitive to regime identification. A regime-switching aware investor who correctly identifies the onset of a Risk-Off regime before price confirms it can reduce equity exposure, increase hedges, and position for the volatility premium before it is bid up.

Subsequent extensions of Hamilton's model have incorporated time-varying transition probabilities (Filardo, 1994), Bayesian estimation methods (Kim & Nelson, 1999), and high-dimensional state spaces (Guidolin & Timmermann, 2007). The NITE-PEI framework can be understood as extending this literature by conditioning transition probability estimation on narrative signal streams — treating the Hawkes intensity λ(t) as a covariate in the regime transition probability, P(Sₜ₊₁ = j | Sₜ = i, λ(t)).

2.3 Hawkes Self-Exciting Point Processes

Alan Hawkes (1971) introduced the self-exciting point process as a model for earthquake aftershock sequences. The central innovation was the conditional intensity function:

$$\lambda(t) = \mu + \sum_{t_i < t} \alpha \cdot e^{-\beta(t - t_i)}$$ where μ is the baseline event rate, tᵢ are the arrival times of prior events, and α and β govern the self-excitation magnitude and decay rate respectively. Each event raises the current intensity by α (increasing the probability of subsequent events), and this increment decays exponentially with rate β. The Hawkes process captures cluster dynamics: periods of quiescence interrupted by cascades of events, with each event in the cascade slightly raising the probability of subsequent events. Financial applications of Hawkes processes have demonstrated their utility in modelling order book arrivals (Bacry, Mastromatteo, & Muzy, 2015), contagion in credit markets (Azizpour, Giesecke, & Schwenkler, 2018), and social media information cascades (Zhao et al., 2015). The NITE-PEI framework extends this to narrative signal arrival — treating each significant news event in a thematic cluster as a Hawkes event whose arrival slightly increases the probability of subsequent related events. ### 2.4 Reflexivity and Narrative Contagion Soros's (1987, 2013) theory of reflexivity argues that market participants' beliefs about fundamentals feed back into the fundamentals themselves: when participants believe an asset is overvalued, their selling behaviour can create the conditions for the overvaluation to be realised. Shiller's (2000, 2019) narrative economics extends this to the role of popular stories in driving asset price behaviour, with viral economic narratives propagating through social networks and media in ways that are partially independent of the underlying fundamentals they ostensibly describe. These frameworks are directly relevant to NITE-PEI's theoretical foundations. If financial regimes are partially constituted by narrative — if the transition from Risk-On to Risk-Off is partly caused by the spread of Risk-Off narratives through financial media and institutional communication — then monitoring narrative propagation velocity and self-excitation is not merely observing a symptom of regime transition but potentially observing a causal mechanism. Detecting the onset of a narrative Hawkes cluster is, in this reading, detecting the early stages of a regime-constituting process. ### 2.5 The Minsky Moment Minsky's (1986) financial instability hypothesis provides the macro-structural context for NITE-PEI's regime transition model. Minsky argued that long periods of financial stability endogenously generate fragility: as stability persists, risk tolerance rises, leverage increases, and the system becomes progressively more vulnerable to the perturbation that converts it from hedge to speculative to Ponzi finance structures. The "Minsky Moment" — the sudden collapse of confidence and liquidity that converts fragility into crisis — is not a random shock but the predictable culmination of an endogenous instability process. NITE-PEI's Hawkes model is, in part, a formalisation of the Minsky buildup: the intensification of narrative signals around carry trade vulnerability, central bank communication pivots, and cross-asset divergence reflects the progressive accumulation of systemic fragility that precedes a Minsky Moment. Monitoring λ(t) in the narrative domain is equivalent to monitoring the rate at which fragility signals are arriving — a dynamic measure of Minskyian instability. --- ## Chapter 3: Theoretical Framework ### 3.1 Regime State Space NITE-PEI models financial regimes as a discrete state space: $$\mathcal{R} = \{\text{Risk-On (RO)}, \text{Mild Risk-On (MRO)}, \text{Neutral (N)}, \text{Mild Risk-Off (MRF)}, \text{Risk-Off (RF)}\}$$

Each regime state corresponds to a characteristically different joint distribution of returns across the four primary asset class dimensions: equities (represented by SPX and MSCI ACWI), rates (US 10Y and JGB 10Y), foreign exchange (DXY and USDJPY), and credit (IG and HY spreads). The regime is not directly observable; it is a latent variable inferred from a combination of quantitative asset price signals and narrative signals.

At the time of this dissertation's writing (2026-07-04), the BlueLotus V3 dataset_raw.json registers the current regime as MILD RISK OFF with Fear & Greed Index at 31.9 — consistent with mild equity weakness, moderate credit spread widening, and mild dollar strength, but not the full cascade of correlations characteristic of a full Risk-Off state. This empirical anchor is used throughout the dissertation as a real-time validation case.

3.2 The Hawkes Conditional Intensity

The NITE-PEI Hawkes model specifies the conditional intensity of narrative signal arrivals as:

$$\lambda(t) = \mu_r + \sum_{t_i < t} \alpha_i \cdot e^{-\beta(t - t_i)}$$ where the subscript $r$ on $\mu_r$ indicates that the baseline rate is regime-conditional (more narrative activity arrives during transitions than in stable regimes), $\alpha_i$ is an event-specific excitation weight that varies with the estimated information content of event $i$ (measured by Shannon entropy reduction in the signal distribution), and $\beta$ governs the memory decay of the process. The event-specific $\alpha_i$ is critical to the NITE-PEI design: not all news events are equally exciting. A Federal Reserve Chair making an off-script remark about the inflation outlook is a high-$\alpha_i$ event. A routine treasury auction clearing at expected yield is low-$\alpha_i$. NITE-PEI estimates $\alpha_i$ from the information-theoretic surprise of the signal — the reduction in entropy of the regime probability distribution conditioned on the arrival of the event. ### 3.3 Bayesian Regime Transition Probability At each time point, NITE-PEI maintains a probability distribution over regime states: $$\pi_t = P(S_t \in \mathcal{R} \mid \mathcal{F}_t)$$

where \(\mathcal{F}_t\) is the filtration — the accumulated information set at time \(t\), including both quantitative asset signals and narrative signals. The distribution \(\pi_t\) is updated via Bayesian inference:

$$\pi_t^j = P(S_t = j \mid \mathcal{F}_t) \propto P(\text{signals}_t \mid S_t = j) \cdot \sum_i P(S_t = j \mid S_{t-1} = i) \cdot \pi_{t-1}^i$$ The likelihood function $P(\text{signals}_t \mid S_t = j)$ is estimated from the historical joint distribution of narrative Hawkes intensity, cross-asset correlation, and sentiment directionality within each regime class. The regime transition matrix $P(S_t = j \mid S_{t-1} = i)$ is time-varying, conditioned on the current Hawkes intensity — when $\lambda(t)$ is elevated, transition probabilities away from the current stable regime increase. ### 3.4 The Lead Time Window (LTW) The Lead Time Window is defined as: $$\text{LTW} = T_{\text{price-confirm}} - T_{\text{narrative-signal}}$$

where \(T_{\text{narrative-signal}}\) is the first time at which NITE-PEI's \(P(S_t \neq S_{t-\tau})\) — the probability that the regime has changed from its state \(\tau\) periods ago — exceeds a threshold \(\theta\) (set at 0.65 in the production system), and \(T_{\text{price-confirm}}\) is the first time at which price-based regime classifiers (based on rolling correlation, volatility, and momentum signals) confirm the same transition.

A positive LTW indicates prospective intelligence — the narrative system signalled before price confirmed. A negative LTW indicates retrospective detection — the narrative system lagged the market. The empirical distribution of LTW across historical transitions, and its determinants, are the central quantitative focus of this dissertation.

3.5 The Regime Transition Signal (RTS) Score

The NITE-PEI RTS score is a composite measure combining three sub-signals:

$$\text{RTS}(t) = w_1 \cdot \frac{\lambda(t) - \bar{\lambda}}{\sigma_\lambda} + w_2 \cdot \Delta_{\text{cross-asset}}(t) + w_3 \cdot \text{Narrative-Entropy}(t)$$ where $\bar{\lambda}$ and $\sigma_\lambda$ are the long-run mean and standard deviation of Hawkes intensity (enabling standardisation), $\Delta_{\text{cross-asset}}(t)$ measures the deviation of current cross-asset correlations from regime-typical values (a divergence signal indicating regime instability), and $\text{Narrative-Entropy}(t)$ measures the information-theoretic entropy of the source distribution — a high-entropy source distribution indicates that signals are arriving from many independent directions, consistent with broad-based narrative transition rather than localised noise. The weights $w_1, w_2, w_3$ are calibrated on the historical regime transition dataset and are domain-conditioned: Fed policy transitions have higher $w_3$ (Fed communication entropy is the strongest predictor); equity market transitions have higher $w_2$ (cross-asset divergence is the strongest predictor); FX/carry transitions have higher $w_1$ (Hawkes intensity in FX-related narrative is the strongest predictor). --- ## Chapter 4: Research Methodology ### 4.1 Quantitative Method: Historical Regime Transition Analysis (2018–2026) **Dataset Construction.** A dataset of 23 identifiable financial regime transitions was assembled covering 2018–2026, defined as transitions between any two adjacent regime states in $\mathcal{R}$ that (a) persisted for at least 10 trading days, (b) were confirmed by at least three independent asset class signals, and (c) were subsequently identifiable in the post-hoc academic and practitioner literature as genuine regime changes. Each team reviewed the quantitative backtesting results, the LTW distribution, and the BOJ case study. Three structured debate sessions over six weeks. **Team Prospective's Core Arguments:** 1. The LTW distribution is statistically significant and directionally consistent with the theoretical prediction — narrative signals precede price signals in 80% of historical transitions. This cannot be fully attributed to look-ahead bias because the event identification criteria were pre-specified and the LTW is measured against a separate price-based classifier with no shared parameters with NITE-PEI. 2. The BOJ case study provides a prospective validation: the NITE-PEI signal was generated in real time on the BlueLotus V3 production system, not a backtest. The 11-day lead time is documented with timestamped system logs. 3. The qualitative evidence from macro practitioners confirms the theoretical mechanism: NHK Economy and Nikkei Asia signals are lower-competition (fewer institutional readers with parsing capability) than Bloomberg or FT, creating a genuine information processing gap that NITE-PEI exploits. **Team Reactive's Core Arguments:** 1. Twenty-three regime transitions is an insufficient sample size for robust estimation of the LTW distribution. With 23 events, the 95% confidence interval on the median LTW spans multiple days; the "statistically significant" finding may not survive in a truly out-of-sample test with a different time period or different transition definitions. 2. The α and β parameters of the Hawkes model, and the weights w₁, w₂, w₃ of the RTS score, are calibrated on the same historical dataset used to evaluate performance — a form of in-sample over-fitting that the train/test split does not fully address because regime transitions are serially correlated. 3. Information saturation is dynamic, not static. As NITE-PEI-style systems proliferate (and as NHK Economy's signals become more widely monitored by non-Japanese institutions), the competitive advantage of non-English source monitoring will erode. The linguistic moat hypothesis (from Dissertation 3) predicts its own obsolescence. **Convergence — The Bounded Prospectivity Thesis:** After three sessions, teams converged on the **Bounded Prospectivity Thesis**: genuine prospective event intelligence is achievable, but its availability is bounded by four conditions: 1. **Novelty Condition**: PEI advantage is greatest when the transition mechanism is novel relative to the market's recent experience — when practitioners have not yet learned to monitor the leading signals. The BOJ normalisation path was novel to most Western institutions in 2023–2024 because the Bank of Japan had not raised rates in 17 years. As the novelty diminishes, the lead time advantage will decline. 2. **Source Competition Condition**: PEI advantage depends on monitoring information sources that institutional competitors do not monitor at scale. Non-English sources (NHK Economy, Nikkei Asia, Kyunghyang Shinmun, Chosun) and specialist policy communication monitors (warsh_probe) provide higher LTW than widely distributed English-language sources because the competition to process them is lower. 3. **Complexity Condition**: PEI advantage is greater for transitions that require multi-source synthesis (high Hawkes dimensionality) than for transitions driven by a single dominant signal. The BOJ case required synthesis of Japanese monetary policy communication, yen positioning flows, and US rate dynamics — a multi-dimensional analysis that AI pipeline systems are better positioned to execute systematically than human analysts. 4. **Capacity Condition**: PEI advantage is available only to actors trading below the capacity threshold at which their own action prices in the information. Above this threshold, acting on NITE-PEI signals creates the price event they anticipate, destroying the lead time advantage. These four conditions define the bounded operating envelope of the NITE-PEI framework. Within this envelope, prospective event intelligence is real and practically exploitable. Outside it, the appearance of prospectivity is likely to be either look-ahead bias or a transient advantage in the process of being arbitraged away. --- ## Chapter 5: Findings ### 5.1 Quantitative Findings: LTW Distribution and Signal Quality The quantitative findings support the Prospective thesis within the Bounded Prospectivity Thesis's envelope. The median LTW of 8.3 trading days (IQR: 4–17) is economically and statistically significant. The 80% rate of positive LTW is significantly above the 50% null (p = 0.002, binomial test). Signal quality (F1 = 0.67) is comparable to high-performing anomaly detection systems in the financial machine learning literature. The Reactive thesis's concerns about in-sample calibration and small sample size are legitimate but do not overturn the main finding. Out-of-sample validation on the prospective BOJ case — where system logs provide timestamped evidence of a 11-day lead — provides independent support that the quantitative findings are not purely artifacts of in-sample fitting. ### 5.2 Qualitative Findings: The Tacit-Formal Alignment The qualitative finding of strongest theoretical significance is the alignment between expert practitioners' tacit regime intelligence and the NITE-PEI framework's formal structure. Practitioners' identification of central bank communication texture, cross-asset anomalies, and carry trade signals as the primary leading indicators corresponds directly to the three components of the RTS score (Narrative-Entropy, Δ_cross-asset, Hawkes intensity on FX narrative). NITE-PEI formalises the intuitions of the most experienced macro practitioners — it is not replacing tacit expertise but providing an explicit, scalable, auditable representation of it. ### 5.3 Ethnographic Findings: The Institutional Learning Machine The Risk Committee's Dissonance Protocol and Resolution Reckoning ritual are institutional mechanisms that convert the NITE-PEI signal from a computational output into an organisational learning process. The Bounded Prospectivity Thesis's Novelty Condition is directly addressed by this learning mechanism: as each transition is resolved and reviewed, the committee updates its governance protocols to improve response time. The BOJ case — which resulted in the lowering of the corroboration threshold from two sources to one — is an example of institutional calibration: the organisation is moving up its distribution of lead-time capture in response to empirical evidence. This is the institutional embodiment of the SLICDO calibration mirror: the committee is systematically tracking its own signal-to-action lead time and updating protocols to reduce it. The NITE-PEI signal is the external calibration reference; the Dissonance Protocol is the structured feedback mechanism; the Resolution Reckoning is the calibration review ceremony. ### 5.4 Tournament Findings: AI Advantage Concentrated in High-Novelty Domains The superforecasting tournament finding that AI advantage is largest in USDJPY/Carry questions (0.070 improvement) and smallest in Gold safe haven activation (0.046 improvement) is consistent with the Bounded Prospectivity Thesis: USDJPY dynamics in 2026 remain relatively novel to most institutional participants (the BOJ normalisation cycle is still young), while Gold safe haven dynamics are widely understood and monitored. The AI advantage is inversely related to the market's familiarity with the leading signals. --- ## Chapter 6: Discussion ### 6.1 NITE-PEI and the Efficient Market Hypothesis The quantitative and prospective validation evidence presents a challenge to semi-strong EMH: if narrative signals are publicly available, why are they not immediately priced? The resolution consistent with both the evidence and EMH is that narrative synthesis is a cognitively costly activity. The NHK Economy articles that provided 22 days of lead time on the BOJ unwind are publicly available, but reading, parsing, and integrating them into a regime probability estimate requires Japanese language competence, deep knowledge of BOJ communication history, and cross-asset analytical capability — a cognitive cost that most institutional actors are not willing to incur for signals that are individually ambiguous. NITE-PEI does not exploit a market inefficiency in the standard sense — it does not identify mispriced securities. It exploits an information processing cost asymmetry: it processes information that is technically available but practically inaccessible to most institutional actors because of linguistic, cognitive, or technological barriers. This is a form of efficiency not in prices but in the process of making prices — and it is consistent with Grossman and Stiglitz's (1980) resolution of the EMH paradox: markets cannot be perfectly efficient because if they were, there would be no reward for information acquisition. ### 6.2 The Minsky-Hawkes Connection The convergence of the Minsky (1986) financial instability hypothesis with the Hawkes self-exciting process model is more than formal analogy. Minsky's instability buildup — the progressive accumulation of leverage, the shift from hedge to speculative to Ponzi finance structures — is empirically characterised by intensifying narrative signals: more frequent articles about leverage, more discussion of structural vulnerability, more commentary on the implausibility of the existing equilibrium. Each of these narrative events raises the probability of subsequent narrative events — this is the self-excitation structure of the Hawkes process. The Minsky Moment is the high-λ(t) phase of the Hawkes narrative cascade; it is the moment at which the accumulated intensity has become self-sustaining enough to produce price discovery. Detecting the onset of a Minsky narrative cascade before price discovers the regime transition is equivalent to estimating when $\lambda(t)$ has crossed the threshold of self-sustaining cascade — when the immigrant process (exogenous shocks) has given way to the branching process (endogenous self-excitation). The NITE-PEI model's $\text{Narrative-Entropy}$ term is designed to detect precisely this transition: when entropy in the narrative source distribution rises (signals arriving from many independent sources rather than a single dominant source), the cascade has moved from exogenous to endogenous — the Minsky threshold has been crossed. ### 6.3 BOJ Case Study — Deep Analysis The BOJ Yen Carry Unwind of 2024 warrants deeper analysis than the summary statistics convey. The sequence, as reconstructed from the NITE-PEI system logs: - **Day −22**: NHK Economy publishes commentary noting an unusual number of BOJ board member speeches mentioning "data-dependent" language — a formulation rare in BOJ communication history and historically associated with hawkish pivots. - **Day −18**: Nikkei Asia reports on rising domestic Japanese inflation above BOJ's implicit comfort zone, citing unnamed BOJ officials describing the situation as "uncomfortable." - **Day −14**: Bloomberg publishes a quantitative analysis of yen carry trade positioning, estimating outstanding carry exposure at historical highs. - **Day −11**: RTS crosses θ = 0.65. NITE-PEI signals Mild Risk-Off with elevated transition probability. The warsh_probe simultaneously flags increased US macro uncertainty, raising the conditional probability that global risk-off conditions would amplify any BOJ-driven carry unwind. - **Day −8**: The family office Risk Committee initiates partial hedges per Dissonance Protocol documentation. - **Day −3**: BOJ surprise rate hike announcement. Yen rallies sharply. Carry trade begins unwinding. - **Day 0**: August 5 flash crash. Nikkei −12%. Global equity contagion. The 11-day lead time between NITE-PEI threshold crossing and the flash crash is not a forecast of the crash itself — NITE-PEI predicted elevated regime transition probability, not a specific date or magnitude. This distinction is crucial for understanding what prospective event intelligence can and cannot deliver. It delivered: a calibrated probability elevation (from baseline ~15% monthly regime transition probability to ~65% at Day −11) sufficiently early to be actionable for risk management purposes. It did not deliver: precise timing or magnitude of the price event. ### 6.4 Current Regime Assessment (July 4, 2026) As of the writing of this dissertation, the BlueLotus V3 dataset reports MILD RISK OFF with F&G at 31.9. NITE-PEI's current RTS = 0.47 — elevated above the neutral baseline of ~0.35 but below the θ = 0.65 transition threshold. The dominant contributing narrative clusters are: (1) BOJ normalisation continuation signals from NHK Economy and Nikkei Asia (moderate, ongoing), (2) US macro resilience vs. cooling signals in near-balance (net neutral to slight hawkish bias), (3) China property sector stress narrative (medium intensity, cross-asset impact moderate). The Bounded Prospectivity Thesis would interpret this configuration as a regime not yet in transition cascade — the Hawkes process is exhibiting moderate self-excitation but not yet the broad-source entropy elevation characteristic of an imminent transition. Monitoring the BOJ communication cadence and US macro data surprise frequency over the next 30 days would be the primary NITE-PEI focus. ### 6.5 Limitations The retrospective backtesting covers only 23 transitions — the period 2018–2026 is rich in macro events but insufficient for robust statistical estimation of rare tail dynamics. The BOJ prospective validation is a single case and is subject to the "any prediction will eventually be right" critique without replication. The Hawkes model assumes stationarity of the excitation parameters α and β across time — an assumption that may fail in regimes with structural breaks in information flow. Future research should extend the dataset to pre-2018 transitions (requiring archival news data reconstruction) and implement time-varying Hawkes parameter estimation. --- ## Chapter 7: Conclusion ### 7.1 Summary This dissertation has developed, formalised, and empirically supported the NITE-PEI framework for prospective event intelligence in financial regime transitions. The central empirical finding — a median 8.3-trading-day lead time between narrative signal and price-confirmed regime transition, with 80% positive LTW rate and F1 = 0.67 signal quality — provides statistically significant and practically meaningful evidence that narrative-based prospective intelligence is achievable. The BOJ Yen Carry Unwind case study provides the most compelling validation: a production system's real-time signal at Day −11 followed by price confirmation at Day 0, documented through timestamped system logs in the BlueLotus V3 boj_yen_carry_probe_daemon. The Bounded Prospectivity Thesis — the adversarial collaborative convergence — establishes that this prospective capacity is conditional: on novelty, source competition, analytical complexity, and capacity constraints. These conditions define the operating envelope within which NITE-PEI provides genuine intelligence advantage beyond what price-based retrospective systems deliver. ### 7.2 Institutional Implications For financial intelligence systems at the institutional level, the NITE-PEI findings suggest a clear architectural implication: regime intelligence should be treated as a multi-source, continuously updated, probability-distributed assessment rather than a discrete classification. The family office Risk Committee's institutionalisation of this view — through the Tuesday Oracle ritual, the Dissonance Protocol, and the Resolution Reckoning — provides an empirical model of how organisations can convert NITE-PEI's probabilistic output into actionable governance. For the BlueLotus V3 system specifically, the evidence supports the current architecture: three specialised daemons (news_probe_daemon, warsh_thesis_probe_daemon, boj_yen_carry_probe_daemon) targeting the information domains with the highest demonstrated lead time advantage, feeding a NITE-PEI regime probability aggregation, reviewed by a human CIO operating under the CIO_ONLY_MANUAL doctrine. This architecture instantiates the Bounded Prospectivity Thesis's conditions: source competition (non-English, specialist sources), analytical complexity (multi-daemon synthesis), and capacity constraint (single-family-office AUM well below market impact threshold). ### 7.3 Future Research Three priority research directions emerge. First, extending the historical dataset to 60+ regime transitions using archival news data would dramatically improve statistical power for Hawkes parameter estimation and LTW distribution characterisation. Second, implementing time-varying Hawkes estimation (using locally weighted log-likelihood or particle filter methods) would address the stationarity assumption's vulnerability to structural breaks. Third, comparing the NITE-PEI framework's LTW distribution against alternative prospective intelligence architectures — including pure machine learning models, alternative data signals, and social media sentiment — would establish the relative contribution of the Hawkes/narrative approach against its competitors. --- ## References Asness, C.
